🕵️ Markov Switching — Regime Detective

Markets seem to live in regimes: long calm spells with small steady gains, punctuated by crisis spells with violent swings. A Markov-switching model formalises this: an unobserved state st ∈ {1, 2} follows a Markov chain with transition probabilities p11 and p22, and returns have regime-specific mean μs and volatility σs. This page gives you the parameter cards, seven interactive platforms (simulate regimes, run the Hamilton filter live, dissect the transition matrix, tell Markov switching from GARCH, step through the filter one Bayes update at a time, choose the number of regimes, and forecast regime probabilities), a clinic of classic estimation problems, the Regime Detective game, and twelve code challenges.

Platform 2 — Hamilton filter, live

The Hamilton filter is just Bayes' rule applied one observation at a time: predict where the chain probably is, then re-weight by how likely today's return is under each regime. Step through a simulated series (n = 150; μ₁ = 0.8, σ₁ = 2, μ₂ = −0.5, p₁₁ = 0.97 fixed) and watch the filtered probability update. At the end, the Kim smoother is overlaid so you can see what hindsight buys you.

Platform 4 — Regime vs GARCH face-off

Platform 5 — Hamilton filter step-through (Bayes in slow motion)

Platform 7 — Regime forecasting lab

Suppose the filter says the probability of crisis today is P₀. What is the probability of crisis at horizon h? Just push the belief through the transition matrix h times: P(st+h) = P₀·Ph. The deviation from the ergodic distribution shrinks by λ₂ = p₁₁ + p₂₂ − 1 each period — regime knowledge has a half-life. The forecast mean is the probability-weighted mix of the two regime means (μ₁ = 0.8, σ₁ = 2, μ₂ = −0.5, σ₂ = 6 fixed here).
