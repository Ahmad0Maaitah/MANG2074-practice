MANG2074 · Financial Econometrics 1Week 7 — ARCH & GARCH
← All topics

Financial returns are roughly unpredictable in their mean — but wildly predictable in their variance: calm days cluster with calm days, crises with crises. This week you drive a GARCH(1,1) volatility engine, see why the ACF of r² (not r) exposes ARCH effects, and learn to spot crisis-prone markets from the returns alone.

📚 Parameters & concepts

The GARCH cast. Click a card for the formula, the values real markets produce, the extremes, and the estimation traps.

▾ detailsωVariance constant
The baseline drip of variance that keeps σ² from collapsing to zero.
Meaning. The floor-setting intercept of the variance equation; with α and β it pins down the long-run variance.
Formula. σ²t = ω + α·r²t−1 + β·σ²t−1; long-run σ̄² = ω/(1−α−β).
Typical values. Tiny: for daily % returns with σ̄ ≈ 1% and persistence 0.97, ω ≈ 0.03. Scale-dependent — multiply returns by 100 and ω changes by 10,000×.
At the extremes. ω → 0 with α+β < 1: variance withers towards zero (degenerate). ω large: a high volatility floor that clustering can never dip below.
Estimation problems. Weakly identified when α+β ≈ 1 (the long-run level barely binds in-sample); optimisers drive ω̂ to its bound. The standard fix is variance targeting: fix σ̄² at the sample variance and estimate only α, β.
▾ detailsαARCH / reaction
How violently volatility reacts to yesterday’s news.
Meaning. The weight on yesterday’s squared return — the “news” term. Big α = jumpy, spiky volatility.
Formula. The α·r²t−1 term; it sets the curvature of the news-impact curve (Platform 2).
Typical values. 0.05–0.15 for daily equity indices; higher for commodities and crypto.
At the extremes. α = 0: volatility ignores news entirely and just decays deterministically. α large: kurtosis explodes — the fourth moment only exists if 3α² + 2αβ + β² < 1.
Estimation problems. α̂ and β̂ trade off along the α+β ridge (their estimates are strongly negatively correlated); with Student-t errors α̂ typically drops because the t soaks up some outliers.
▾ detailsβGARCH / memory
How much of yesterday’s variance carries straight into today.
Meaning. The autoregressive memory of the variance process — what makes calm and stormy spells persist.
Formula. The β·σ²t−1 term. Substituting recursively: today’s variance is a geometrically weighted average of ALL past squared returns with weights αβj.
Typical values. 0.85–0.95 on daily data — memory dominates reaction.
At the extremes. β = 0: pure ARCH(1), which needs many lags to fit real data (the historical reason GARCH won). β → 1: volatility becomes a slow-moving, almost deterministic level.
Estimation problems. β̂ collapses or hits 1 when ω is mis-scaled or a variance break is present; always check the data scale (% vs decimal) before blaming the optimiser.
▾ detailsα+βPersistence
The AR(1) coefficient of the variance process — how slowly vol shocks fade.
Meaning. The single most-watched number in any GARCH output: the speed of mean reversion in risk.
Formula. E[σ²t+k] − σ̄² = (α+β)k(σ²t − σ̄²) — exponential decay at rate α+β (Platform 4 runs on exactly this).
Typical values. 0.95–0.99 on daily equity data. 0.9723 ≈ 0.5: a 23-day half-life.
At the extremes. α+β ≥ 1: IGARCH territory — variance shocks never die and σ̄² does not exist. α+β ≈ 0: white noise, nothing for GARCH to do.
Estimation problems. Unmodelled breaks in the variance LEVEL masquerade as α+β ≈ 1 (Lamoureux–Lastrapes) — “spurious IGARCH” is the week’s clinic classic.
▾ detailst½Half-life of volatility
Days until half of a volatility shock has decayed away.
Meaning. The practical translation of persistence: how long after a crisis does the market stay scared?
Formula. t½ = ln(½)/ln(α+β).
Typical values. α+β = 0.95 → 13.5 days; 0.98 → 34 days; 0.99 → 69 days.
At the extremes. α+β → 1: half-life → ∞ (shocks permanent); α+β = 0.5: half-life of one day — clusters you can barely see.
Estimation problems. Brutally sensitive to persistence near 1: moving α̂+β̂ from 0.98 to 0.99 doubles the half-life, and the confidence interval around it is enormous.
▾ detailsσ̄²Unconditional variance ω/(1−α−β)
The long-run anchor that every variance forecast converges to.
Meaning. The average variance over a long horizon — the level the term structure of volatility forecasts flattens onto.
Formula. σ̄² = ω/(1−α−β); k-step forecast: σ²t+k = σ̄² + (α+β)k(σ²t − σ̄²).
Typical values. Daily equity index: σ̄ ≈ 0.8–1.5%; annualised by √252 that is roughly 13–24%.
At the extremes. Denominator → 0: σ̄² explodes (and at α+β = 1 it does not exist at all). With ω̂ ≈ 0 the implied σ̄ can come out absurdly small — sanity-check it against the sample sd.
Estimation problems. A ratio of two noisy estimates is itself noisy; variance targeting replaces it with the sample variance, usually a more stable choice for forecasting.
▾ detailsνStudent-t degrees of freedom
How fat the tails of the standardised shocks are — beyond what GARCH explains.
Meaning. Even after GARCH filtering, real standardised returns have fat tails; ν measures how fat. Small ν = wild tails.
Formula. zt ~ standardised t(ν); kurtosis = 3 + 6/(ν−4) for ν > 4.
Typical values. ν̂ = 4–8 on daily equity returns; ν̂ > 30 is effectively Gaussian.
At the extremes. ν → ∞: the normal. ν ≤ 4: infinite kurtosis; ν ≤ 2: infinite variance — the model’s scaling breaks down.
Estimation problems. ν̂ is pinned down by a handful of extreme days, so it is fragile in short samples; it also trades off against α (both produce fat tails) — comparing fitted ν across subsamples is a good stability check.
▾ detailsγGJR asymmetry / leverage
The extra kick that NEGATIVE shocks give to volatility.
Meaning. Markets fall harder than they rise: bad news raises future volatility more than good news of the same size (the leverage effect). γ measures the asymmetry.
Formula. GJR: σ²t = ω + (α + γ·1[rt−1<0])·r²t−1 + β·σ²t−1; persistence becomes α + γ/2 + β.
Typical values. Equity indices: γ̂ ≈ 0.05–0.15, often LARGER than α̂ — the asymmetry is not a side dish.
At the extremes. γ = 0: back to symmetric GARCH. α + γ/2 + β ≥ 1: non-stationary variance. γ < 0 (rallies scarier than crashes) shows up for some commodities.
Estimation problems. Identified mainly by crash days, so short calm samples cannot find it; sign asymmetry and size asymmetry get confounded — compare GJR against EGARCH (Platform 2) as a robustness check.

Asymmetry lab — news-impact curves & the leverage effect

A news-impact curve plots tomorrow’s variance σ²t+1 against today’s return, holding σ²t at its long-run level (= 1 here). Symmetric GARCH treats −2% and +2% identically; GJR adds γ only when the return is negative; EGARCH makes the response exponential and asymmetric. Base parameters: α = 0.04, β = 0.82; the symmetric model gets αsym = α + γ/2 (what a symmetric fit would average out to on asymmetric data); the EGARCH curve is calibrated to match the others at zero — illustrative, not estimated.   2. γ = 0.24: a −2% day now hits tomorrow’s variance 7× harder than a +2% day (check the ratio chip) — that asymmetry is what equity options desks call “the skew”.
  3. Press 🎲 a few times at γ = 0.15 and watch the highlighted worst day: the volatility line jumps hardest exactly after the big negative return — never after an equally big positive one.
  4. Why “leverage”? A price fall raises a firm’s debt/equity ratio, making the equity riskier — plus risk-aversion feedback. The name stuck even though leverage is only part of the story.

ARCH-LM testing bench — size and power, live

Engle’s ARCH-LM test: regress r²t on its own first 5 lags and compute LM = T·R² ~ χ²(5) under H₀: no ARCH. We Monte-Carlo it: 300 simulated samples from your chosen DGP, testing each at 5%. The bars accumulate so you can compare DGPs and sample sizes.

What to notice. Run the bench for each DGP — the bars stay on screen so you can compare.
🧪 Try this.
  1. iid, n = 300: the rejection rate sits near 5% — the test’s SIZE. A test that rejects a true H₀ more than its nominal level is broken.
  2. High persistence, n = 300: rejection (POWER) jumps to ~90%. Volatility clustering this strong is hard to miss.
  3. High persistence, n = 100: power collapses to ~20% — in short samples, failing the ARCH-LM test is NOT evidence of no ARCH.
  4. Low persistence, n = 1000: weak clustering becomes detectable once the sample is long enough — power rises with both persistence and n.

⚠️ Problems clinic

Six ways GARCH modelling goes wrong in practice. Click each card; three have live demos.

▾ openThe IGARCH boundary: α+β ≥ 1
Persistence estimates that hit 1 — and what dies when they do.
What it is. At α+β = 1 the unconditional variance ω/(1−α−β) no longer exists; variance shocks are permanent and the k-step forecast grows linearly: σ²t+k = σ²t + kω.
How it bites. Long-horizon risk numbers explode or become meaningless; optimisers stall on the boundary with non-convergence warnings; capital calculations downstream inherit the nonsense.
How to detect. α̂+β̂ ≥ 0.999, parameters pinned at constraint bounds, enormous standard errors on ω̂; persistence that JUMPS when a crisis enters the estimation window.
Remedy. First suspect a variance break (see below) rather than true IGARCH; try Student-t errors; use variance targeting; or accept the EWMA/RiskMetrics special case (ω = 0, α+β = 1) knowingly, for short horizons only.
▾ openω ≈ 0 and variance targeting
The optimiser drives ω̂ to zero and the long-run variance becomes fiction.
What it is. When persistence is high, the likelihood is almost flat in ω — the sample barely visits the long run, so the long-run parameter is weakly identified.
How it bites. Implied σ̄ = √(ω̂/(1−α̂−β̂)) comes out at silly values (0.01% or 400% annualised); long-horizon forecasts anchor on fiction; refitting tomorrow gives a different fiction.
How to detect. ω̂ at its lower bound with a huge standard error; implied σ̄ wildly different from the sample standard deviation; results that change with the data’s scaling (% vs decimals).
Remedy. Variance targeting: set ω = σ̂²sample(1−α−β) and estimate only α and β; always feed GARCH percentage returns (scale ≈ 1), not raw decimals.
▾ openSquared returns as a “realised vol” proxy
r²ₜ is an unbiased — but spectacularly noisy — estimate of σ²ₜ.
What it is. r²t = σ²tz²t, and z² has mean 1 but variance 2: the proxy’s noise dwarfs the signal on any given day.
How it bites. Regress r² on a GARCH forecast and the R² is 5–10% even when the GARCH model is EXACTLY right (Andersen & Bollerslev 1998). Managers junk good models because the evaluation proxy is rubbish.
How to detect. Run the demo: the gold true σ² is smooth, the blue r² is a hailstorm around it. If your “model vs realised” scatter looks like that, blame the proxy first.
Remedy. Evaluate against realised variance built from intraday data (sum of 5-minute squared returns), use QLIKE loss rather than MSE, and never judge a vol model by its R² against r² alone.
▾ openNormal errors underestimate the tails
GARCH explains a lot of the fat tails — but never all of them.
What it is. Even with time-varying σ, the standardised residuals ẑ = r/σ̂ of real returns are leptokurtic: extreme days are more common than the normal allows.
How it bites. Gaussian-GARCH 99% VaR gets breached roughly twice as often as designed (week 8 will count them); tail events get declared “impossible” right up until they happen.
How to detect. Kurtosis of standardised residuals > 3; QQ-plot tails peeling away; Jarque–Bera rejecting on ẑ; 99% backtest breaches ≫ 1%.
Remedy. Estimate with Student-t errors (dist='t' / distribution.model="std") and let the data pick ν̂; for the deepest tail, extreme-value methods on the standardised residuals.
▾ openStructural breaks inflating persistence
A one-off shift in the variance LEVEL masquerades as α+β ≈ 1.
What it is. Splice a calm regime onto a stormy one and the model can only reconcile them with near-permanent persistence (Lamoureux & Lastrapes 1990). The ACF of r² decays slowly even when, within each regime, returns are iid.
How it bites. Estimate over 2019–2021 and α̂+β̂ ≈ 0.999: risk forecasts stay crisis-level for years; half-life estimates become “forever”.
How to detect. Subsample estimates differ sharply; persistence jumps when a crisis window enters; break tests (ICSS) on squared returns flag variance change-points.
Remedy. Allow a break (dummies, split samples), use shorter/rolling estimation windows, or regime-switching volatility models (week 9).
▾ openGARCH on low-frequency data
Monthly returns barely cluster — aggregation kills the ARCH effect.
What it is. Temporal aggregation averages volatility clustering away: daily GARCH(1,1) with persistence 0.97 implies only weak monthly ARCH effects, and quarterly ones are nearly invisible.
How it bites. Fitting GARCH to a fund’s 120 monthly observations: α̂ ≈ 0 or wildly unstable, β̂ unidentified, and the “volatility forecasts” are noise dressed as science.
How to detect. ARCH-LM on the monthly returns is insignificant (try Platform 3 with small n and weak persistence — that is the monthly world); parameter estimates flip sign across subsamples.
Remedy. Estimate volatility on daily (or intraday) data and aggregate the forecasts up; at genuinely low frequencies, accept a constant-variance model — an honest σ̂ beats a fictional GARCH.
