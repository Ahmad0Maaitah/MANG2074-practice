MANG2074 · Financial Econometrics 1Week 7 — ARCH & GARCH
← All topics

Financial returns are roughly unpredictable in their mean — but wildly predictable in their variance: calm days cluster with calm days, crises with crises. This week you drive a GARCH(1,1) volatility engine, see why the ACF of r² (not r) exposes ARCH effects, and learn to spot crisis-prone markets from the returns alone.

📚 Parameters & concepts

The GARCH cast. Click a card for the formula, the values real markets produce, the extremes, and the estimation traps.

▾ detailsωVariance constant
The baseline drip of variance that keeps σ² from collapsing to zero.
Meaning. The floor-setting intercept of the variance equation; with α and β it pins down the long-run variance.
Formula. σ²t = ω + α·r²t−1 + β·σ²t−1; long-run σ̄² = ω/(1−α−β).
Typical values. Tiny: for daily % returns with σ̄ ≈ 1% and persistence 0.97, ω ≈ 0.03. Scale-dependent — multiply returns by 100 and ω changes by 10,000×.
At the extremes. ω → 0 with α+β < 1: variance withers towards zero (degenerate). ω large: a high volatility floor that clustering can never dip below.
Estimation problems. Weakly identified when α+β ≈ 1 (the long-run level barely binds in-sample); optimisers drive ω̂ to its bound. The standard fix is variance targeting: fix σ̄² at the sample variance and estimate only α, β.
▾ detailsαARCH / reaction
How violently volatility reacts to yesterday’s news.
Meaning. The weight on yesterday’s squared return — the “news” term. Big α = jumpy, spiky volatility.
Formula. The α·r²t−1 term; it sets the curvature of the news-impact curve (Platform 2).
Typical values. 0.05–0.15 for daily equity indices; higher for commodities and crypto.
At the extremes. α = 0: volatility ignores news entirely and just decays deterministically. α large: kurtosis explodes — the fourth moment only exists if 3α² + 2αβ + β² < 1.
Estimation problems. α̂ and β̂ trade off along the α+β ridge (their estimates are strongly negatively correlated); with Student-t errors α̂ typically drops because the t soaks up some outliers.
▾ detailsβGARCH / memory
How much of yesterday’s variance carries straight into today.
Meaning. The autoregressive memory of the variance process — what makes calm and stormy spells persist.
Formula. The β·σ²t−1 term. Substituting recursively: today’s variance is a geometrically weighted average of ALL past squared returns with weights αβj.
Typical values. 0.85–0.95 on daily data — memory dominates reaction.
At the extremes. β = 0: pure ARCH(1), which needs many lags to fit real data (the historical reason GARCH won). β → 1: volatility becomes a slow-moving, almost deterministic level.
Estimation problems. β̂ collapses or hits 1 when ω is mis-scaled or a variance break is present; always check the data scale (% vs decimal) before blaming the optimiser.
▾ detailsα+βPersistence
The AR(1) coefficient of the variance process — how slowly vol shocks fade.
Meaning. The single most-watched number in any GARCH output: the speed of mean reversion in risk.
Formula. E[σ²t+k] − σ̄² = (α+β)k(σ²t − σ̄²) — exponential decay at rate α+β (Platform 4 runs on exactly this).
Typical values. 0.95–0.99 on daily equity data. 0.9723 ≈ 0.5: a 23-day half-life.
At the extremes. α+β ≥ 1: IGARCH territory — variance shocks never die and σ̄² does not exist. α+β ≈ 0: white noise, nothing for GARCH to do.
Estimation problems. Unmodelled breaks in the variance LEVEL masquerade as α+β ≈ 1 (Lamoureux–Lastrapes) — “spurious IGARCH” is the week’s clinic classic.
▾ detailst½Half-life of volatility
Days until half of a volatility shock has decayed away.
Meaning. The practical translation of persistence: how long after a crisis does the market stay scared?
Formula. t½ = ln(½)/ln(α+β).
Typical values. α+β = 0.95 → 13.5 days; 0.98 → 34 days; 0.99 → 69 days.
At the extremes. α+β → 1: half-life → ∞ (shocks permanent); α+β = 0.5: half-life of one day — clusters you can barely see.
Estimation problems. Brutally sensitive to persistence near 1: moving α̂+β̂ from 0.98 to 0.99 doubles the half-life, and the confidence interval around it is enormous.
▾ detailsσ̄²Unconditional variance ω/(1−α−β)
The long-run anchor that every variance forecast converges to.
Meaning. The average variance over a long horizon — the level the term structure of volatility forecasts flattens onto.
Formula. σ̄² = ω/(1−α−β); k-step forecast: σ²t+k = σ̄² + (α+β)k(σ²t − σ̄²).
Typical values. Daily equity index: σ̄ ≈ 0.8–1.5%; annualised by √252 that is roughly 13–24%.
At the extremes. Denominator → 0: σ̄² explodes (and at α+β = 1 it does not exist at all). With ω̂ ≈ 0 the implied σ̄ can come out absurdly small — sanity-check it against the sample sd.
Estimation problems. A ratio of two noisy estimates is itself noisy; variance targeting replaces it with the sample variance, usually a more stable choice for forecasting.
▾ detailsνStudent-t degrees of freedom
How fat the tails of the standardised shocks are — beyond what GARCH explains.
Meaning. Even after GARCH filtering, real standardised returns have fat tails; ν measures how fat. Small ν = wild tails.
Formula. zt ~ standardised t(ν); kurtosis = 3 + 6/(ν−4) for ν > 4.
Typical values. ν̂ = 4–8 on daily equity returns; ν̂ > 30 is effectively Gaussian.
At the extremes. ν → ∞: the normal. ν ≤ 4: infinite kurtosis; ν ≤ 2: infinite variance — the model’s scaling breaks down.
Estimation problems. ν̂ is pinned down by a handful of extreme days, so it is fragile in short samples; it also trades off against α (both produce fat tails) — comparing fitted ν across subsamples is a good stability check.
▾ detailsγGJR asymmetry / leverage
The extra kick that NEGATIVE shocks give to volatility.
Meaning. Markets fall harder than they rise: bad news raises future volatility more than good news of the same size (the leverage effect). γ measures the asymmetry.
Formula. GJR: σ²t = ω + (α + γ·1[rt−1<0])·r²t−1 + β·σ²t−1; persistence becomes α + γ/2 + β.
Typical values. Equity indices: γ̂ ≈ 0.05–0.15, often LARGER than α̂ — the asymmetry is not a side dish.
At the extremes. γ = 0: back to symmetric GARCH. α + γ/2 + β ≥ 1: non-stationary variance. γ < 0 (rallies scarier than crashes) shows up for some commodities.
Estimation problems. Identified mainly by crash days, so short calm samples cannot find it; sign asymmetry and size asymmetry get confounded — compare GJR against EGARCH (Platform 2) as a robustness check.
▾ detailsztStandardised residuals
What is left after dividing each return by its conditional σ — the model’s report card.
Meaning. ẑt = ε̂t/σ̂t. If the variance equation is RIGHT, the ẑt are iid with mean 0 and variance 1 — all the clustering has been absorbed by σ̂t. Every GARCH diagnostic is some question asked of ẑ.
Formula. zt = εt/σt; post-fit checks: Ljung–Box on ẑ²t (no clustering left), ARCH-LM on ẑt, kurtosis and QQ-plot of ẑt (distribution check).
Typical values. Sample sd of ẑ ≈ 1 by construction; kurtosis of ẑ on daily equity data ≈ 3.5–5 — far below the 8–12 of raw returns (GARCH explains much of the fat tail, never all of it).
At the extremes. Autocorrelation surviving in ẑ²: the variance equation is wrong or incomplete — add asymmetry (GJR) or lags. Kurtosis ≫ 3: the Gaussian likelihood mis-weights the big days — refit with Student-t shocks.
Estimation problems. ẑ uses the ESTIMATED σ̂t, so standard test critical values are only approximate; the first few ẑ depend on how σ²₀ was initialised; and one mis-modelled crash day can dominate every moment of ẑ.
▾ detailsℓ(θ)Log-likelihood & MLE mechanics
The score that GARCH estimation climbs — there is no OLS formula, only a numerical mountain.
Meaning. For each candidate (ω,α,β) the computer rebuilds the entire σ²t recursion, scores how plausible the data are, and an optimiser climbs to the maximum. OLS has a formula; GARCH has a hill-climb (Platform 5 draws the hill).
Formula. ℓ = −½Σt[ln 2π + ln σ²t + ε²t/σ²t], with σ²t = ω + αε²t−1 + βσ²t−1 started from some σ²₀ (usually the sample variance).
Typical values. Scale-dependent and meaningless alone — only DIFFERENCES matter: model comparisons use ℓ via AIC/BIC, and a likelihood-ratio test is 2Δℓ ~ χ².
At the extremes. Near α+β = 1 the surface becomes a long flat ridge — many (α,β) pairs fit almost equally well, so optimisers wander and report huge standard errors. At parameter bounds (ω → 0, α → 0) you get “convergence failed” warnings.
Estimation problems. Different packages use different σ²₀ rules, optimisers and constraint handling — so arch, rugarch, EViews and Stata give slightly DIFFERENT estimates on identical data. Not a bug: a flat ridge plus numerical optimisation. Rescale returns to % (×100) before fitting or the optimiser works on absurdly tiny numbers.
▾ detailsλGARCH-in-mean (risk premium)
Pays you for holding variance: the risk-return tradeoff wired into the mean equation.
Meaning. Merton’s logic: if risk is predictable (GARCH says it is), expected return should be higher when predicted variance is higher. GARCH-M puts σ²t straight into the mean equation and λ prices it.
Formula. rt = μ + λσ²t + εt, εt = σtzt (variants use λσt or λ ln σ²t — and λ̂ changes with the choice, which is itself a warning sign).
Typical values. Theory suggests λ ≈ 2–4 (a relative-risk-aversion number). Estimates in the literature: anywhere from −2 to +6 and usually insignificant — French–Schwert–Stambaugh (1987) found weakly positive, Glosten–Jagannathan–Runkle (1993) negative.
At the extremes. λ = 0: the mean and variance equations separate — plain GARCH. λ < 0: “volatility feedback” — vol rises depress prices on impact, dragging the in-sample relation negative even if the true premium is positive.
Estimation problems. Brutally weak identification: the premium λσ²t is a few basis points a day while the noise σtzt is ~1% — Platform 6 lets you feel how little a t-stat can see. λ̂ is unstable across samples, frequencies and functional forms.
▾ detailsω*Variance targeting
Fix ω from the sample variance instead of estimating it — one less parameter, far fewer tears.
Meaning. Instead of letting the optimiser hunt for ω (which it does badly when persistence is high), pin the model’s long-run variance to the sample variance and estimate only α and β.
Formula. ω = σ̂²sample(1−α−β), so that ω/(1−α−β) = σ̂²sample by construction. In code: variance.targeting=TRUE (rugarch) or fixing ω manually (arch).
Typical values. Standard practice on risk desks and in RiskMetrics-style systems: the long-run anchor comes from a moment estimate (reliable), the dynamics from the likelihood (the part it identifies well). Platform 5 uses it to reduce the MLE surface to two dimensions.
At the extremes. As α+β → 1 targeting keeps σ̄² sane (free MLE lets it explode or vanish). If the sample variance is contaminated — a crisis or a level break in the window — the target itself is wrong, and every long-horizon forecast anchors on the contamination.
Estimation problems. Slightly less efficient than full MLE when the model is true; standard errors should account for the pre-estimated σ̂² (software often does not); but for FORECASTING it usually wins on stability — the classic bias/variance trade.
▾ detailsRVtVolatility proxies & realised variance
σ²ₜ is never observed: r²ₜ is an honest but wild guess at it; intraday RV is a sharp one.
Meaning. To EVALUATE a volatility forecast you must compare it with something observable. The daily squared return is unbiased for σ²t but extremely noisy; realised variance — the sum of squared INTRADAY returns — is dramatically more accurate.
Formula. r²t = σ²tz²t: E[r²t] = σ²t but Var(r²t) = 2σ⁴t. RVt = Σj=1..m r²t,j over m intraday intervals: error variance ≈ 2σ⁴t/m — shrinking like 1/m.
Typical values. The workhorse is 5-minute RV (m = 78 in US equity hours). Mincer–Zarnowitz check: regress the proxy on the forecast, want intercept 0 and slope 1; against r² even a PERFECT GARCH forecast scores R² ≈ 5–10% (Andersen & Bollerslev 1998) — against RV the same forecast scores 50%+. Platform 7 reproduces both numbers.
At the extremes. m → ∞: in theory RV → the true integrated variance; in practice microstructure noise (bid–ask bounce, discrete ticks) biases RV upward below ~1–5 minute sampling, so m is capped in real markets.
Estimation problems. Judging vol models by R² against r² junks good models — the proxy, not the forecast, is the problem; prefer RV and robust losses (QLIKE) over MSE; and remember RV needs clean, liquid intraday data — for small caps or long histories you are stuck with noisy proxies.

MLE mountain — where GARCH estimates actually come from

There is no OLS formula for GARCH. For every candidate (α,β) the software rebuilds the whole σ²ₜ recursion and scores it with the Gaussian log-likelihood ℓ = −½Σ[ln2π + lnσ²ₜ + r²ₜ/σ²ₜ], with ω set by variance targeting so the surface is two-dimensional. GARCH-in-mean & risk-return lab

Merton’s tradeoff, simulated: rₜ = μ + λσ²ₜ + σₜzₜ (λ in the usual decimal-units convention, so with % returns the daily drift is λσ²ₜ/100). We then try to recover λ the way a two-step practitioner would: OLS of rₜ on σ²ₜ — and we even hand the regression the TRUE σ²ₜ, a luxury real researchers never get (they must use a first-stage GARCH σ̂²ₜ; full MLE estimates λ jointly). The t-stat uses White heteroskedasticity-robust standard errors, because the error variance IS σ²ₜ by construction. Calibration: a deliberately volatile single asset, σ̄ = 2.5%/day (≈ 40%/yr) — the tradeoff is far harder to detect on a calm index.

⚠️ Problems clinic

Ten ways GARCH modelling goes wrong in practice. Click each card; five have live demos.

▾ openThe IGARCH boundary: α+β ≥ 1
Persistence estimates that hit 1 — and what dies when they do.
What it is. At α+β = 1 the unconditional variance ω/(1−α−β) no longer exists; variance shocks are permanent and the k-step forecast grows linearly: σ²t+k = σ²t + kω.
How it bites. Long-horizon risk numbers explode or become meaningless; optimisers stall on the boundary with non-convergence warnings; capital calculations downstream inherit the nonsense.
How to detect. α̂+β̂ ≥ 0.999, parameters pinned at constraint bounds, enormous standard errors on ω̂; persistence that JUMPS when a crisis enters the estimation window.
Remedy. First suspect a variance break (see below) rather than true IGARCH; try Student-t errors; use variance targeting; or accept the EWMA/RiskMetrics special case (ω = 0, α+β = 1) knowingly, for short horizons only.
▾ openω ≈ 0 and variance targeting
The optimiser drives ω̂ to zero and the long-run variance becomes fiction.
What it is. When persistence is high, the likelihood is almost flat in ω — the sample barely visits the long run, so the long-run parameter is weakly identified.
How it bites. Implied σ̄ = √(ω̂/(1−α̂−β̂)) comes out at silly values (0.01% or 400% annualised); long-horizon forecasts anchor on fiction; refitting tomorrow gives a different fiction.
How to detect. ω̂ at its lower bound with a huge standard error; implied σ̄ wildly different from the sample standard deviation; results that change with the data’s scaling (% vs decimals).
Remedy. Variance targeting: set ω = σ̂²sample(1−α−β) and estimate only α and β; always feed GARCH percentage returns (scale ≈ 1), not raw decimals.
▾ openSquared returns as a “realised vol” proxy
r²ₜ is an unbiased — but spectacularly noisy — estimate of σ²ₜ.
What it is. r²t = σ²tz²t, and z² has mean 1 but variance 2: the proxy’s noise dwarfs the signal on any given day.
How it bites. Regress r² on a GARCH forecast and the R² is 5–10% even when the GARCH model is EXACTLY right (Andersen & Bollerslev 1998). Managers junk good models because the evaluation proxy is rubbish.
How to detect. Run the demo: the gold true σ² is smooth, the blue r² is a hailstorm around it. If your “model vs realised” scatter looks like that, blame the proxy first.
Remedy. Evaluate against realised variance built from intraday data (sum of 5-minute squared returns), use QLIKE loss rather than MSE, and never judge a vol model by its R² against r² alone.
▾ openNormal errors underestimate the tails
GARCH explains a lot of the fat tails — but never all of them.
What it is. Even with time-varying σ, the standardised residuals ẑ = r/σ̂ of real returns are leptokurtic: extreme days are more common than the normal allows.
How it bites. Gaussian-GARCH 99% VaR gets breached roughly twice as often as designed (week 8 will count them); tail events get declared “impossible” right up until they happen.
How to detect. Kurtosis of standardised residuals > 3; QQ-plot tails peeling away; Jarque–Bera rejecting on ẑ; 99% backtest breaches ≫ 1%.
Remedy. Estimate with Student-t errors (dist='t' / distribution.model="std") and let the data pick ν̂; for the deepest tail, extreme-value methods on the standardised residuals.
▾ openStructural breaks inflating persistence
A one-off shift in the variance LEVEL masquerades as α+β ≈ 1.
What it is. Splice a calm regime onto a stormy one and the model can only reconcile them with near-permanent persistence (Lamoureux & Lastrapes 1990). The ACF of r² decays slowly even when, within each regime, returns are iid.
How it bites. Estimate over 2019–2021 and α̂+β̂ ≈ 0.999: risk forecasts stay crisis-level for years; half-life estimates become “forever”.
How to detect. Subsample estimates differ sharply; persistence jumps when a crisis window enters; break tests (ICSS) on squared returns flag variance change-points.
Remedy. Allow a break (dummies, split samples), use shorter/rolling estimation windows, or regime-switching volatility models (week 9).
▾ openGARCH on low-frequency data
Monthly returns barely cluster — aggregation kills the ARCH effect.
What it is. Temporal aggregation averages volatility clustering away: daily GARCH(1,1) with persistence 0.97 implies only weak monthly ARCH effects, and quarterly ones are nearly invisible.
How it bites. Fitting GARCH to a fund’s 120 monthly observations: α̂ ≈ 0 or wildly unstable, β̂ unidentified, and the “volatility forecasts” are noise dressed as science.
How to detect. ARCH-LM on the monthly returns is insignificant (try Platform 3 with small n and weak persistence — that is the monthly world); parameter estimates flip sign across subsamples.
Remedy. Estimate volatility on daily (or intraday) data and aggregate the forecasts up; at genuinely low frequencies, accept a constant-variance model — an honest σ̂ beats a fictional GARCH.
▾ openOptimiser non-convergence & starting values
“Warning: failure to converge” — and an α̂ of exactly 0.000.
What it is. GARCH is fitted by numerically climbing the log-likelihood (Platform 5). The surface has flat ridges, boundaries (ω, α, β ≥ 0; α+β < 1) and the occasional local maximum — so the climber can stall, hit a wall, or stop somewhere that depends on where it started.
How it bites. Symptoms in real output: α̂ = 0 or β̂ = 0 sitting EXACTLY on the constraint boundary; enormous or NaN standard errors; estimates that change when you change the starting values or feed the same data in a different scale; a different answer from arch vs rugarch vs EViews on identical data.
How to detect. Read the convergence flag (everyone scrolls past it); refit from 2–3 different starting values and compare ℓ — if the “optimum” moves, you never found it; check whether any parameter sits exactly on a bound.
Remedy. (1) Rescale returns to percentages (×100) — the single most common fix; (2) variance targeting to remove ω from the search; (3) sensible starts (α = 0.05, β = 0.90); (4) try a different optimiser/solver; (5) if α̂ + β̂ keeps pinning to 1, suspect a variance break, not the optimiser.
▾ openThe explosive region: α+β > 1
Strictly past the IGARCH knife-edge, variance does not just persist — it EXPLODES.
What it is. The knife-edge α+β = 1 (clinic card above) means shocks never die. Strictly ABOVE 1, E[σ²ₜ] grows geometrically at rate (α+β)ᵗ: the variance process is explosive, and k-step forecasts diverge to infinity instead of flattening onto a long-run level.
How it bites. Simulation and stress-testing engines fed α+β > 1 produce paths whose vol doubles, quadruples, then overflows — risk numbers grow with the horizon without bound. Estimates just above 1 sometimes sneak through unconstrained optimisers on short crisis samples, then quietly poison every downstream forecast.
How to detect. Run the demo: at α+β = 1.05 the (log-scale!) σ²ₜ path climbs orders of magnitude in 200 days while the stationary 0.97 twin mean-reverts. In estimates: persistence > 1 with a tiny sample or unconstrained solver; forecast curves that slope UP forever.
Remedy. Impose the stationarity constraint in estimation (most packages do by default — check); re-estimate on a longer window; if persistence insists on ≥ 1, model the break or regime explicitly rather than letting the variance equation explode.
▾ openIgnoring leverage on equity indices
Fitting symmetric GARCH to a stock index systematically under-forecasts post-crash volatility.
What it is. Equity volatility responds asymmetrically: a −2% day raises tomorrow’s variance far more than a +2% day (the leverage effect — see the γ concept card and Platform 2). Symmetric GARCH forces one α on both, averaging the asymmetry away.
How it bites. After a crash the symmetric model under-predicts the volatility that actually follows — VaR set too low precisely after the market has fallen, margin calls and hedges sized off stale σ̂. After a rally it over-predicts instead. On index data γ̂ is often LARGER than α̂, so this is not a refinement — it is half the news term.
How to detect. Engle–Ng sign-bias test: regress squared standardised residuals ẑ² on a dummy for negative lagged returns (and its interactions) — significance means the symmetric model left sign information on the table. Quick check: correlate ẑ²ₜ with 1[rₜ₋₁<0].
Remedy. Fit GJR (o=1 in arch, "gjrGARCH" in rugarch) or EGARCH and compare news-impact curves in Platform 2; keep the asymmetric model if γ̂ is significant — on equity indices it almost always is.
▾ openParameter uncertainty in volatility forecasts
Your forecast curve is a POINT estimate — drawn with parameters you do not actually know.
What it is. The k-step forecast σ̄² + (α+β)ᵏ(σ²ₜ − σ̄²) is evaluated at α̂, β̂, ω̂ — each with sampling error. Because persistence enters as a POWER, tiny errors in α̂+β̂ compound brutally with horizon: at k = 60, persistence 0.95 vs 0.97 differ by a factor of (0.97/0.95)⁶⁰ ≈ 3.5 in the decay term.
How it bites. Two analysts fit the same model to overlapping windows, get α̂+β̂ of 0.94 and 0.97 — both within each other’s confidence intervals — and publish 1-month vol forecasts that differ by a third. Long-horizon “term structures of risk” get quoted to two decimals with no bands at all.
How to detect. Look at the standard errors on α̂ and β̂ (and their strong negative correlation — the MLE-mountain ridge); small n means wide intervals on persistence, hence enormous intervals on anything at k ≫ 1. The demo draws the forecast fan from 24 statistically-equivalent persistence draws.
Remedy. Report interval forecasts: delta-method standard errors for σ̂²ₜ₊ₖ (name-check — the derivative of the forecast in θ̂), or simulate/bootstrap parameter draws and redraw the curve; shrink the parameter count (variance targeting); be honest that beyond a month the data barely discriminate.
