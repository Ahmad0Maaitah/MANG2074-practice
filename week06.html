A model is only as good as its out-of-sample forecasts. Here you split a 20-year monthly series (synthetic UK-house-price-style growth) into training and hold-out samples, watch dynamic and static forecasts behave very differently, and judge models by RMSE and MAE — then go head-to-head against an AR(1) in the Forecast Duel.

📚 Parameters & concepts

The vocabulary of forecast evaluation. Click any card for the formula, real-market values, the extremes, and the estimation traps.

▾ detailshForecast horizon
How many steps ahead you are predicting.
Meaning. The distance between the information you have (time t) and the thing you want (time t+h). Everything about forecast quality degrades in h.
Formula. ŷt+h|t = E[yt+h | Ωt]; for an AR(1): μ + φh(yt − μ).
Typical values. Risk desks: h = 1–10 days. Macro: 1–8 quarters. Equity-return “forecasts” beyond a few days: effectively the unconditional mean.
At the extremes. h = 1: dynamics matter most. h → ∞: the forecast collapses to the mean and the error variance to the unconditional variance — the model adds nothing.
Estimation problems. h-step errors overlap and are serially correlated (MA(h−1) even for a perfect model), so naive standard errors and tests on them are wrong.
▾ detailsT₀Training window / split
The data your parameters are allowed to see; the rest is the hold-out.
Meaning. Estimation uses {1…T₀}; honest evaluation uses {T₀+1…T} only. The split is the firewall between fitting and testing.
Formula. RMSEoos computed over t = T₀+1…T with parameters frozen (or re-estimated using only data ≤ t).
Typical values. 70–80% train; finance backtests roll 250–1000-day windows.
At the extremes. Window too short: parameter noise dominates. Too long: averages over regime changes (see Platform 4). No hold-out at all: you are reporting in-sample fit, which is marketing, not evidence.
Estimation problems. A single split is one random draw — rankings can flip with the split point. Use rolling-origin evaluation; never shuffle time-series data.
▾ detailsRMSERMSE & MAE
Average miss size — RMSE punishes big misses quadratically, MAE doesn’t.
Meaning. The two standard loss summaries of forecast errors et = yt − ŷt.
Formula. RMSE = √(mean e²); MAE = mean |e|. Always RMSE ≥ MAE.
Typical values. A useless model’s RMSE ≈ the standard deviation of the target — that is the bar to beat, and for returns it is barely beatable.
At the extremes. RMSE ≫ MAE: a few catastrophic misses (fat-tailed errors). RMSE mysteriously below the in-sample σ̂: smell look-ahead bias.
Estimation problems. Both are scale-dependent: an RMSE of 2 means nothing without the target’s scale, and comparing RMSEs across different samples or periods is meaningless (see clinic).
▾ details±1.96σ̂hForecast interval
The range that should contain the outcome 95% of the time — if the model is right.
Meaning. A point forecast plus honesty about its precision. Central banks publish these as fan charts.
Formula. ŷt+h ± 1.96·σ̂e(h), with σ̂e²(h) = σ̂²·Σj<hψj² for an ARMA.
Typical values. Width grows with h and flattens at ±1.96 × unconditional sd — the fan you saw in Platform 1.
At the extremes. h = 1: width ±1.96σ̂. h → ∞: the widest the fan ever gets. If intervals do NOT widen with h, the model thinks it can see the future — be suspicious.
Estimation problems. Standard intervals ignore parameter and model uncertainty, so true coverage < 95%; with fat-tailed financial errors they are narrower still than they should be.
▾ detailsdyn/statDynamic vs static forecasts
Feed your own forecasts back in (dynamic) — or peek at actual data each step (static).
Meaning. Dynamic = true multi-step forecasting: ŷt+2 uses ŷt+1. Static = a sequence of one-step forecasts using actual lags.
Formula. Dynamic: ŷt+2 = ĉ + φ̂ŷt+1. Static: ŷt+2 = ĉ + φ̂yt+1.
Typical values. Static errors stay ≈ σ̂ at all “horizons”; dynamic errors grow with h.
At the extremes. Static forecasts hug the data and look brilliant — they are answering an easier question. Dynamic forecasts decay to the mean — that humility is correct.
Estimation problems. Quoting static accuracy as if it were multi-step performance is one of the oldest over-claims in applied forecasting. Always say which you computed.
▾ detailsψjψ-weights (impulse responses)
The MA(∞) weights that build the forecast-error variance.
Meaning. ψj is how much a shock from j periods ago still moves today’s value — the building blocks of the fan chart.
Formula. yt = μ + Σψjεt−j; σe²(h) = σ²·Σj=0h−1ψj². AR(1): ψj = φj.
Typical values. Equity returns: ψ’s die almost instantly. Rates and volatility: slow geometric decay.
At the extremes. Σψj² < ∞ ⇔ stationary. Random walk: ψj = 1 forever, so σe²(h) = hσ² grows without bound — intervals never stop widening.
Estimation problems. ψ̂’s inherit the parameter noise of φ̂ and θ̂, and the errors compound as you iterate them out to long horizons.
▾ detailsαSES smoothing constant
How fast exponential smoothing forgets the past.
Meaning. Simple exponential smoothing forecasts a flat line at a level that is a geometrically weighted average of history — α sets the forgetting rate.
Formula. ℓt = αyt + (1−α)ℓt−1; weight on the observation j periods back: α(1−α)j. SES ≡ ARIMA(0,1,1) in disguise.
Typical values. 0.1–0.3 for macro series; RiskMetrics’ famous λ = 0.94 for variance is the same idea with α = 0.06.
At the extremes. α → 1: the naive last-value forecast (random-walk behaviour). α → 0: the long-run average of everything ever seen.
Estimation problems. An optimised α̂ near 1 is the data telling you the series is close to a random walk; α̂ is poorly pinned down when the series is short or the level barely moves.
▾ detailsDMDiebold–Mariano test
Is model 1 genuinely more accurate than model 2 — or just lucky this sample?
Meaning. A t-test on the loss differential between two forecast series. The antidote to “my RMSE is 0.02 smaller, ship it”.
Formula. dt = e²1t − e²2t; DM = d̄ / √(LRV(d̄)/n) → N(0,1) under H₀: equal accuracy.
Typical values. |DM| > 1.96 rejects at 5%. Sobering fact: most published model comparisons fail to reject — accuracy differences are usually within noise.
At the extremes. Short evaluation windows: hopeless power. h > 1: overlapping errors require a HAC variance. Nested models: use the Clark–West correction.
Estimation problems. Running DM on the best of fifty tried models is data snooping — the test is only honest for one pre-registered comparison. Feel its size and its power for yourself in Platform 5.
▾ detailsL(e)Loss functions
What does a miss cost? Your loss function defines the forecast you should make.
Meaning. Forecast evaluation always assumes a loss L(e). MSE punishes big misses quadratically, MAE linearly, and asymmetric losses punish one sign of error more than the other — and the optimal forecast changes with the loss.
Formula. MSE: L = e²; MAE: L = |e|; linex: L(e) = exp(ae) − ae − 1 (a > 0 makes under-prediction much costlier than over-prediction).
Typical values. Under MSE the optimal forecast is the conditional mean; under MAE the conditional median; under linex, mean + (a/2)·σ² — a deliberately biased forecast is optimal. A central bank that under-predicts inflation (and so tightens too late) suffers more than one that over-predicts: its rational forecast should over-shoot slightly.
At the extremes. a → 0: linex collapses to MSE/2 and the bias vanishes. Large a or huge forecast variance: the optimal “forecast” sits far from the mean on purpose. RMSE ≫ MAE in your evaluation: a handful of catastrophic misses dominate — exactly the misses a quadratic loss screams about and a linear loss shrugs at.
Estimation problems. Model rankings can flip when the loss changes — model A can win on RMSE and lose on MAE on the same hold-out. Never report a forecast comparison without saying which loss it is a comparison under.
▾ detailswiForecast combination
Average several imperfect forecasts — the simple average is brutally hard to beat.
Meaning. Combine rival forecasts into fc = Σwifi (Σwi = 1). Different models make different mistakes; averaging diversifies their idiosyncratic errors away exactly like a portfolio diversifies idiosyncratic risk.
Formula. Bates–Granger optimal weights minimise w'Σ̂w over the simplex, where Σ̂ is the forecast-error covariance matrix; with uncorrelated errors wi* ∝ 1/MSEi.
Typical values. In empirical horse-races (Stock–Watson and decades since), the equal-weight combination beats most individual models and usually beats the “optimal” estimated weights — the forecast combination puzzle.
At the extremes. All weight on one model: combination degenerates to model selection, inheriting its full selection risk. Negative weights: you are shorting a forecaster — occasionally optimal in-sample, almost always fragile out-of-sample.
Estimation problems. Optimal weights need Σ̂ estimated from a short history of forecast errors; the estimation noise in ŵ usually costs more out-of-sample than clever weighting gains. That is the puzzle’s resolution — see it happen in Platform 6.
▾ details80%→?Interval & density forecasts
A point forecast without a band is half an answer.
Meaning. An interval forecast attaches a stated probability to a range; a density forecast hands over the whole predictive distribution. The Bank of England’s inflation fan charts are density forecasts drawn as nested bands.
Formula. Empirical coverage = (1/n)Σ 1(yt ∈ bandt), which should ≈ the nominal level; a Gaussian 80% one-step band is ŷ ± 1.28σ̂.
Typical values. Central banks publish 30/60/90% fans; risk desks live and die by 95% and 99% VaR bands (week 8). Backtested coverage within a couple of points of nominal is respectable.
At the extremes. Under-coverage: bands too narrow — the model is over-confident and reality keeps “surprising” it. Over-coverage: bands so wide they say nothing. Breaches that cluster in time: total coverage can look fine while the dynamics are wrong — the deadliest failure mode.
Estimation problems. Fat tails and volatility clustering both wreck Gaussian bands, in different ways; calibration is an empirical claim that must be backtested, never assumed. Platform 7 is a coverage backtest you can drive; Christoffersen’s test (week 8) formalises it.
▾ detailst|T₀Pseudo-out-of-sample design
“Pretend it’s 2019 and forecast 2020” — honest only if you fake the past carefully.
Meaning. Replay history: estimate using data up to t, forecast t+h, roll the origin forward, collect the errors. The three re-estimation schemes: recursive (expanding window), rolling (fixed length W), fixed (estimate once, never update).
Formula. Recursive: θ̂t from {1…t}; rolling: from {t−W+1…t}. Errors et+h = yt+h − ŷt+h|t(θ̂t).
Typical values. 70–80% of the sample for the initial estimation window; banks roll 250–1000-day windows. Results should be reported across several split points, not one.
At the extremes. One split is one random draw — put the split just before a crisis and again just after, and watch the model ranking flip. A tiny hold-out cannot rank models; a tiny estimation window cannot pin parameters down.
Estimation problems. Pseudo-OOS is still not real time: today’s data file contains revised macro numbers, delisted stocks have vanished, and — worst — you chose the model after seeing the full sample. True real-time evaluation uses vintage data and a pre-registered model. Pseudo-OOS is the honest minimum, not the gold standard.

🔬 Platforms — seven forecasting workbenches

Out-of-sample test bench

Data: monthly annual-growth series, Jan 2005 – Dec 2024 (n = 240), generated once from yₜ = 0.5 + 1.1yₜ₋₁ − 0.3yₜ₋₂ + εₜ, σ = 0.8 (unconditional mean 2.5%). The model is estimated by OLS on the training window only.

What to notice.
🧪 Try this.
  1. AR(2), dynamic, split at 2016 (training 144): watch the fan flatten at the unconditional mean — long-horizon forecasts are “the mean, plus humility”.
  2. Same setup, static: the forecast suddenly hugs the data and the band stops widening. It looks better because it answers an easier question — one month ahead, every time.
  3. Mean benchmark, dynamic: at long horizons the gap to the AR(2) is smaller than you would guess. Parsimony is a serious competitor.
  4. Split at 2023 (12-month hold-out): re-run your comparisons — rankings wobble, because 12 observations cannot reliably rank models.

  2. φ = 0.30: the RMSE curve is at the ceiling by h ≈ 3. Macro-growth forecasting, roughly.

Model tournament — 200 series, five forecasters, one leaderboard

Pick the true data-generating process. One click simulates 200 independent series (T = 130); on each, five rival forecasters are estimated on 110 observations and produce dynamic forecasts for the final 20. The leaderboard reports average RMSE across all horizons and replications.

What to notice. Choose a DGP and press 🏁.
🧪 Try this.
  1. DGP = AR(2): the correctly specified AR(2) wins — but look how close AR(1) runs it. Extra correct structure buys surprisingly little.
  2. DGP = random walk: the naive no-change forecast wins and the sample-mean forecast is a disaster — the mean of a random walk is a meaningless number.
  3. DGP = near unit root (φ = 0.98): the WRONG random-walk model beats the CORRECTLY specified AR(1). Estimating φ near 1 costs more than assuming φ = 1 for free — a deep forecasting lesson.
  4. Note SES’s quiet competence everywhere — robustness is why practitioners still love exponential smoothing 70 years on.

Window & re-estimation lab — expanding vs rolling when the world changes

An AR(1) series (T = 300) whose intercept can jump at t = 150, shifting the long-run mean from 0 to 5. From t = 80 we produce one-step forecasts, re-estimating by OLS each period two ways: an expanding window (all history) and a rolling window (last W observations). Watch who recovers after the break.

60
What to notice.
🧪 Try this.
  1. Break on, W = 40: the rolling forecaster recovers within ~40 periods of the break; the expanding one is still dragged towards the dead regime years later.
  2. Break on, W = 120: rolling now adapts much more slowly — window length is a bet on how fast the world changes.
  3. Break off: the expanding window wins (slightly) — more data, less parameter noise. Stability rewards memory; instability punishes it.
  4. This bias–variance trade-off is why banks re-estimate risk models on rolling 250-day windows rather than “all data since 1990”.

Diebold–Mariano test bench — is the RMSE gap real, or luck?

Truth is an AR(2): yₜ = 0.3 + 0.5yₜ₋₁ + φ₂yₜ₋₂ + εₜ (or a pure AR(1) in “equal models” mode, where the rival just adds a junk regressor). Both rivals are estimated once on 600 observations, then produce one-step forecasts over an evaluation window of your chosen length. We test H₀: equal accuracy with dₜ = e²₁ₜ − e²₂ₜ, DM = d̄/SE(d̄) ≈ N(0,1) — then re-run the whole contest 300 times to see the test’s size and power.

0.30 300
What to notice. Press ▶ to run.
🧪 Try this.
  1. φ₂ = 0.05, n = 300: the truth really IS an AR(2), but the RMSE gap is invisible and DM almost never rejects. A true improvement too small to detect — that is low power.
  2. φ₂ = 0.40, n = 300: now the second lag earns its keep — DM rejects in most of the 300 reps. Power needs a real gap and a long window.
  3. Equal models: truth is AR(1) and the rival only adds junk — rejections run at ≈ 5%, the test’s size. (A touch above 5%, in fact: nested rivals inflate DM slightly — the Clark–West correction exists for exactly this.)
  4. φ₂ = 0.40 but n = 50: the SAME real gap is now insignificant in most samples. “Model A beat model B in 2023” is one short window, not evidence.

Forecast combination lab — three rivals, one average

The series has a slowly drifting local mean: yₜ = τₜ + uₜ with τₜ = τₜ₋₁ + 0.08ηₜ and uₜ = 0.5uₜ₋₁ + εₜ. Three forecasters fight over it: an AR(1) estimated once on 100 obs (its fixed mean slowly goes stale), SES (α = 0.2, tracks the drift), and a human analyst with genuine private information reported through bias and noise: fₜ = yₜ + bias + 1.5υₜ. Combination weights are fitted in-sample on K obs and judged on 200 fresh out-of-sample obs.

What to notice.
🧪 Try this.
  1. Crank the analyst’s bias to 2.5, then press 🎯: the fitted weights shove the analyst towards zero and the combo STILL beats the best individual — combination survives a biased input if you downweight it. Equal weights, meanwhile, get poisoned: a third of a big bias is still a bias.
  2. K = 30: “optimal” weights estimated on 30 observations usually LOSE to naive equal weights out of sample. Estimation error in the weights is the heart of the forecast combination puzzle.
  3. K = 300: with ten times the data, the estimated weights approach the true optimum and the puzzle fades. It is a small-sample phenomenon — and forecast-error samples are almost always small.
  4. Back to defaults with equal weights, then hit 🎲 a few times: the equal-weight combo beats ALL three individuals in nearly every sample. Averaging forecasts diversifies idiosyncratic mistakes exactly like a portfolio diversifies idiosyncratic risk.

Interval calibration lab — does your 80% band cover 80%?

An AR(1) (φ = 0.6) is fitted on 150 observations, then makes 300 one-step forecasts, each wearing a nominal 80% interval ŷ ± q·ŝ. You pick what the world is really like — and which quantile q the band uses. Breaches (red) should land ~20% of the time, scattered like white noise. In the t₃ world the scale ŝ is fitted by t₃ maximum likelihood (so the wild days don’t inflate it); elsewhere ŝ is the residual sd.

What to notice. Press ▶ to simulate.
🧪 Try this.
  1. Well-specified world, Gaussian z: empirical coverage ≈ 80%, breaches scattered at random. Memorise this picture — it is what calibration looks like.
  2. t₃ fat tails, Gaussian z: coverage drops to ≈ 70%. The scale ŝ is RIGHT — the tail assumption is wrong. Spot the monster breaches that sail far outside the band.
  3. Same fat-tailed world, t₃ quantile (1.64ŝ instead of 1.28ŝ): coverage restored to ≈ 80%. Matching the quantile to the distribution is the whole game.
  4. Volatility clustering: TOTAL coverage looks fine (~80%), but the breaches arrive in storms — P(breach | breach yesterday) far above 20%. Total coverage can hide broken dynamics; Christoffersen’s test (week 8) catches exactly this, and a GARCH band (week 7) is the real fix.

⚠️ Problems clinic

Eight ways forecast evaluations lie to you. Click each card for the symptoms, the test, and the fix — two have live demos.

▾ openLook-ahead bias
Using information at time t that only became available after t.
What it is. Future data leaking into the information set: standardising with the full-sample mean and sd, using finally-revised macro data, or “forecasting” today’s close with today’s close embedded in an indicator.
How it bites. A backtest Sharpe ratio that doubles, then evaporates the day the strategy trades real money. Quant funds have died of this.
How to detect. Results too good to be true (out-of-sample RMSE below in-sample σ̂ is a red flag); audit every transformation for full-sample statistics; re-run with strictly real-time (vintage) data.
Remedy. A pipeline in which the forecast at t may only touch data with timestamps ≤ t — no exceptions, including the scaling constants. For macro inputs, true real-time evaluation uses data vintages — the numbers as first published, not today’s revised series.
▾ openData snooping in backtests
Try 50 models, report the best — “significance” is then guaranteed and meaningless.
What it is. Selection bias: the maximum of many noisy performance measures is biased upwards even if every model is useless.
How it bites. Of 50 random no-skill strategies, the best one typically shows an impressive in-sample equity curve — and a flat one thereafter. Entire newsletters are built on this. Run 20 specifications, report the winner, and an apparently “significant” DM test against the benchmark is close to guaranteed even when nothing works.
How to detect. Ask “how many things were tried?”; White’s reality check / Hansen’s SPA test correct for the search; an untouched final hold-out exposes the winner.
Remedy. Pre-register the comparison, keep a sacred hold-out sample you use exactly once, and shade every reported Sharpe by the size of the search that produced it.
▾ openIgnoring parameter uncertainty in intervals
Your fan chart pretends φ̂ and σ̂ are the truth. They are estimates.
What it is. The textbook interval ŷ ± 1.96σ̂√(Σψ̂²) conditions on the estimated parameters — the extra noise from estimating them is ignored.
How it bites. Nominal 95% intervals cover perhaps 85–90% in short samples; the understatement is worst exactly when data are scarce and decisions are biggest.
How to detect. Backtest the intervals: count how often outcomes fall outside (should be ≈ 5%); simulate from the fitted model and check coverage.
Remedy. Bootstrap forecast intervals, Bayesian predictive distributions, or at minimum a humility factor — and never quote interval coverage you have not backtested.
▾ openComparing RMSE across different samples
“Model A: RMSE 1.2 (2015–19). Model B: RMSE 2.8 (2020–24). A wins!” — no.
What it is. RMSE is scale- and period-dependent. 2020’s volatility was triple 2017’s, so the comparison measures the period, not the models.
How it bites. Vendor pitches and student dissertations alike: a model evaluated on calm years “beats” one evaluated through a crisis.
How to detect. Always ask: same target, same transformation, same evaluation window? If not, the numbers are incommensurable.
Remedy. Common hold-out period for all candidates; scale-free metrics (Theil’s U = RMSE/RMSEnaive); and a Diebold–Mariano test on the SAME error series.
▾ openStructural breaks
Parameters that shift mid-sample poison forecasts estimated on all of history.
What it is. Means, persistence or variances change (2008, COVID, a new policy regime). A model estimated across the break is a weighted average of two worlds and forecasts neither.
How it bites. The expanding-window forecaster in Platform 4 keeps predicting the OLD mean long after the world moved — systematic, one-sided errors that a desk pays for daily. The best historical model can be the worst future model — forecast rankings are not stable across regimes.
How to detect. CUSUM of forecast errors drifting one way; rolling-window parameter estimates trending; Chow tests at suspected break dates.
Remedy. Rolling windows or exponential down-weighting of old data, break dummies, or regime-switching models (week 9 preview). In production, monitor recursively: track the CUSUM of live forecast errors and re-estimate the moment it starts drifting one way.
▾ openOverfit in-sample ⇒ underperform out-of-sample
Fit always improves with parameters. Forecasting doesn’t.
What it is. Extra lags soak up sampling noise as if it were signal. In-sample σ̂ falls mechanically; out-of-sample RMSE rises once the memorised noise fails to repeat.
How it bites. The AIC-champion ARMA(3,3) from week 5; the 12-feature return regression with R² = 0.3 in-sample and negative out-of-sample R². Overfitted trading models lose real money with great confidence.
How to detect. A widening gap between in-sample fit and pseudo-out-of-sample RMSE as you add parameters; BIC refusing to follow AIC upwards.
Remedy. BIC, hold-out evaluation, rolling-origin cross-validation — and the old rule: when in doubt, leave it out.
▾ openOverlapping observations at long horizons
h-step forecast errors overlap — naive standard errors lie.
What it is. At horizon h, consecutive forecast errors share h−1 of the same future shocks, so even a PERFECT model produces MA(h−1) autocorrelated errors. Any variance formula that assumes independent errors is too small — and every t-statistic built on it is too big.
How it bites. The long-horizon predictability literature: regress 5-year stock returns on the dividend yield using monthly overlapping windows and you can conjure t-stats of 4 from almost nothing — much of the apparent long-run predictability of the 1980s melted under proper inference. The same trap inflates DM tests on h > 1 forecasts.
How to detect. Plot the ACF of your h-step errors: spikes out to lag h−1 are EXPECTED, not a bug. If your software’s standard errors ignored them, your t-statistics are overstated — by up to a factor of √h.
Remedy. HAC variances (Newey–West) with at least h−1 lags, Hansen–Hodrick standard errors, or non-overlapping observations (far fewer data points, but honest ones). forecast::dm.test and a HAC-equipped regression of dₜ on a constant both do this for you.
▾ openBenchmark choice
Beating your own worst model is not skill.
What it is. A forecast evaluation is only as honest as the rivals in it. Leave out the naive no-change forecast and the unconditional mean, and “our model wins” may only mean “our model beat the straw men we chose”.
How it bites. Meese–Rogoff (1983): every fashionable structural exchange-rate model of the era LOST to the naive random walk out of sample — a result that still humbles FX forecasting forty years on. The commercial version: vendors benchmarking against a weak in-house alternative rather than against “do nothing”.
How to detect. Compute Theil’s U = RMSE/RMSEnaive: U > 1 means worse than no-change. Always ask: what is the benchmark, and who picked it? Platform 3’s tournament shows the no-change forecast winning whole classes of DGP outright.
Remedy. Hard-wire the naive forecast and the sample mean into every comparison; report Theil’s U next to RMSE; and run the Diebold–Mariano test against the BENCHMARK, not only against your other pet models.
