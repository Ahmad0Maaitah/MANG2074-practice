AR, MA and ARMA processes each leave a distinctive fingerprint in the ACF and PACF — and reading those fingerprints is how econometricians choose a model. This week you drive the ARMA machine, learn the identification rules, then test yourself by unmasking hidden processes from their correlograms alone. 📚 Parameters & concepts

The cast of characters for this week. Click any card for the formula, real-market values, what happens at the extremes, and how estimation goes wrong.

▾ detailsφAR coefficient
How much of yesterday’s value survives into today.
Meaning. The weight on the lagged value — the memory of the level. It controls how persistent the series is.
Formula. yt = c + φ1yt−1 + εt;  ACF: ρk = φk;  half-life = ln(½)/ln|φ|.
Typical values. Daily equity returns ≈ 0 (markets are nearly efficient); volatility, interest-rate spreads, dividend yields 0.9–0.99; GDP growth and inflation 0.3–0.8.
At the extremes. φ → 0: white noise, no memory. φ → 1: the random walk — shocks never die, variance explodes. φ < 0: zig-zag oscillation. |φ| > 1: explosive (bubble territory).
Estimation problems. OLS φ̂ is biased downwards in small samples (≈ −(1+3φ)/T); near φ = 1 its distribution is non-standard, so use Dickey–Fuller critical values, not the t-table.
▾ detailsθMA coefficient
How much of yesterday’s shock (not value) echoes into today.
Meaning. The weight on the lagged innovation — a one-period echo of news, not of the level itself.
Formula. yt = μ + εt + θ1εt−1;  ρ1 = θ/(1+θ²), ρk = 0 for k > 1.
Typical values. Bid–ask bounce induces MA(1) with θ < 0 in high-frequency returns; news/announcement series 0.2–0.5. Note ρ1 can never exceed 0.5, whatever θ.
At the extremes. θ → 0: white noise. |θ| = 1: the non-invertibility boundary — shocks cannot be recovered from the data. θ and 1/θ give the SAME ACF, so we impose |θ| < 1 to identify the model.
Estimation problems. No closed-form OLS (the shocks are unobserved) — needs iterative ML/CSS; estimates notoriously “pile up” at ±1 in small samples.
▾ detailsp, qModel orders
How many AR lags (p) and MA lags (q) you allow the model.
Meaning. The size of the memory you grant the model. Orders are chosen (Box–Jenkins, information criteria), not estimated.
Formula. ARMA(p,q): yt = c + Σφiyt−i + εt + Σθjεt−j.
Typical values. Financial applications rarely need more than (2,2); daily returns are usually best described by (0,0) plus GARCH in the variance.
At the extremes. Too small: leftover autocorrelation, Ljung–Box rejects. Too large: overfitting, enormous standard errors, near-cancelling AR/MA roots.
Estimation problems. Every step up from (p,q) to (p+1,q+1) risks redundant common factors — the likelihood surface goes flat and the optimiser wanders (see the Problems clinic).
▾ detailsρkAutocorrelation (ACF)
Correlation between yt and its own value k periods ago.
Meaning. The memory fingerprint of the series, lag by lag. The whole Box–Jenkins identification step is reading this plot.
Formula. ρk = Cov(yt, yt−k)/Var(yt); sample bands ±1.96/√n under white noise.
Typical values. |ρ1| < 0.1 for daily stock returns; ≈ 0.99 for price levels (that is the unit root talking, not predictability).
At the extremes. All ρk ≈ 0: white noise. Decay so slow it is almost linear: unit root or structural break. Dead cut-off after lag q: MA(q).
Estimation problems. ρ̂k is biased towards 0 in short samples; the ±1.96/√n bands assume iid data — with ARCH effects they are too narrow and you over-detect “significant” lags.
▾ detailsφkkPartial autocorrelation (PACF)
Correlation at lag k after stripping out the effect of lags 1…k−1.
Meaning. What lag k adds once shorter lags have had their say — the mirror tool to the ACF.
Formula. φkk = the last coefficient when yt is regressed on yt−1…yt−k (computed recursively by Durbin–Levinson).
Typical values. For AR(1) data: one spike ≈ φ at lag 1, then noise inside the bands.
At the extremes. Cuts off dead after lag p: pure AR(p). Tails off gradually: an MA part is present. Both ACF and PACF tailing off: mixed ARMA.
Estimation problems. Same ±1.96/√n bands and the same small-sample noise — with n = 100, anything past lag ~25 is numerology, not evidence.
▾ detailsQLjung–Box statistic
One number asking: is there ANY autocorrelation left, up to lag m?
Meaning. A portmanteau (catch-all) test. On residuals it is the exit exam of an ARMA model: did you capture all the dynamics?
Formula. Q = n(n+2)Σk=1m ρ̂k²/(n−k) ~ χ²(m−p−q) under H0: no autocorrelation.
Typical values. On well-fitted ARMA residuals: p-value > 0.05. On raw daily r²: huge (that is the ARCH effect — week 7).
At the extremes. Q enormous → the model is too small. Q suspiciously near zero → you have probably overfitted and squeezed even the sampling noise out.
Estimation problems. Forgetting to dock the degrees of freedom for the p+q estimated parameters makes the test too lenient; the χ² approximation is shaky for m close to n.
▾ detailsAIC·BICInformation criteria
Fit-versus-parsimony scorecards — always pick the LOWEST.
Meaning. Both reward likelihood and fine extra parameters; they disagree on the fine. R² is useless here (it only ever rises).
Formula. AIC = −2lnL + 2k;  BIC = −2lnL + k·ln(n), with k = number of estimated parameters.
Typical values. With n = 500, BIC charges ln(500) ≈ 6.2 per parameter — over three times AIC’s 2. They agree on clear-cut cases and split on marginal ones.
At the extremes. AIC is not consistent: even as n → ∞ it keeps a positive probability of choosing too-large models. BIC is consistent but can underfit small samples.
Estimation problems. Only comparable on the SAME sample with the SAME differencing — an AIC in levels versus an AIC in differences is a meaningless comparison (different data!).
▾ details|φ|<1Stationarity / unit root
Do shocks die out (stationary) — or last forever (unit root)?
Meaning. Covariance stationarity: constant mean, variance and autocovariances. The precondition for the whole ARMA toolkit — and for the ACF to mean anything.
Formula. All roots of 1 − φ1z − … − φpzp = 0 outside the unit circle; for AR(1) simply |φ| < 1. Var(y) = σ²/(1−φ²).
Typical values. Price levels, exchange rates, GDP: I(1) — difference once. Returns, growth rates, spreads: I(0).
At the extremes. At φ = 1, variance grows like tσ², the ACF dies almost linearly, and t-statistics follow Dickey–Fuller distributions, not the normal. Beyond 1: explosive bubbles.
Estimation problems. Regressing one unit-root series on another gives spurious significance (Platform 4!); ADF tests have low power near φ = 1 — with 5 years of data you cannot tell 0.98 from 1.
▾ details|θ|<1Invertibility & common factors
θ and 1/θ tell identical stories — invertibility picks the sensible one.
Meaning. An MA model is invertible when it can be rewritten as an AR(∞) — i.e. today’s shock can be recovered from the observable past. The catch: θ and 1/θ generate exactly the same autocorrelations (ρ₁ = θ/(1+θ²) = (1/θ)/(1+(1/θ)²)), so the data alone cannot tell them apart — they are observationally equivalent. We impose |θ| < 1 to choose the one with a convergent AR(∞) form (see Platform 7 for the live demo).
Formula. All roots of 1 + θ₁z + … + θqzq = 0 outside the unit circle; for MA(1) simply |θ| < 1. Common factors: (1 − φL)yt = (1 − θL)εt with φ = θ cancels to yt = εt — a redundant ARMA dressed up as a model.
Typical values. Estimation software imposes invertibility automatically, so reported |θ̂| < 1 by construction; bid–ask bounce gives θ ≈ −0.1 to −0.4 in high-frequency returns.
At the extremes. |θ| = 1: the non-invertibility boundary — over-differencing lands you exactly there (θ = −1). θ ≈ −φ in an ARMA(1,1): the AR and MA factors cancel, every ψ-weight ≈ 0, and the “model” is white noise in fancy dress.
Estimation problems. θ̂ piles up at ±1 in small samples; near-cancelling factors flatten the likelihood — huge standard errors, optimiser wandering, non-convergence. Remedy: cut p and q by one each and refit (see the Problems clinic).
▾ detailsψkWold decomposition & impulse responses
Every stationary series is a moving average of all past shocks — ψk is the weight on the shock from k periods ago.
Meaning. Wold (1938): any covariance-stationary series can be written yt = μ + Σk=0∞ ψkεt−k with ψ₀ = 1 — an MA(∞). The ψ-weights ARE the impulse-response function (IRF): how much of a one-unit shock survives after k periods.
Formula. AR(1): ψk = φk (so ψ₃ = 0.8³ = 0.512 when φ = 0.8). ARMA(1,1): ψ₁ = φ + θ, then ψk = φψk−1. Long-run cumulative effect Σψk = (1+θ)/(1−φ). Stationarity ⇔ Σψk² < ∞.
Typical values. Daily equity returns: ψ-weights die essentially at k = 0 (no memory — the EMH benchmark). Volatility and interest-rate spreads: ψ-weights still visible after months.
At the extremes. φ → 1: Σψk → ∞ — the random walk, where ψk = 1 forever and every shock is permanent. φ + θ = 0 in ARMA(1,1): all ψk = 0 for k ≥ 1 — the redundant-model fingerprint.
Estimation problems. ψ̂k are nonlinear functions of φ̂ and θ̂, so standard errors need the delta method; a misspecified small model distorts the entire IRF, not just one coefficient; truncating the MA(∞) too early understates long-run persistence.
▾ detailss = 12Seasonality & SARMA
A yearly pattern in monthly data: ACF spikes at lags 12, 24, 36 that ordinary ARMA terms cannot soak up.
Meaning. Regular within-year structure — the spring/summer moving season in UK house prices, dividend months, year-end window dressing. In the correlogram it shows up as spikes at exact multiples of the period s (12 for monthly, 4 for quarterly).
Formula. SARMA: (1 − φL)(1 − ΦL¹²)yt = (1 + θL)(1 + ΘL¹²)εt — seasonal AR/MA polynomials in L¹² multiply the ordinary ones. Alternatives: 11 monthly dummies, or seasonal differencing Δ₁₂yt = yt − yt−12.
Typical values. This module’s UKHP dataset is the canonical case: an AR(2) fits the monthly growth rates nicely at short lags, yet Ljung–Box still rejects at lag 12 — the leftover seasonal spike (Platform 6 dissects exactly this).
At the extremes. Deterministic season (stable pattern): dummies are the clean fix; Δ₁₂ then over-differences and plants Θ ≈ −1. Stochastic season (pattern itself drifts): Δ₁₂ or seasonal ARMA terms are appropriate. Amplitude → 0: nothing to fix — seasonally adjusting non-seasonal data injects fake negative correlation.
Estimation problems. Δ₁₂ throws away the first 12 observations and changes what you are modelling; dummies assume the pattern never changes; off-the-shelf seasonal adjustment (X-13) can smear structure across months and distort dynamics at exactly the lags you care about.
▾ detailsγADF regression anatomy
The unit-root test is one OLS regression and one t-ratio — with non-standard critical values.
Meaning. The Augmented Dickey–Fuller test runs Δyt = α + βt + γyt−1 + Σi=1pδiΔyt−i + εt and tests H₀: γ = 0 (unit root) against H₁: γ < 0 (stationary). Since γ = φ − 1, “γ = 0” is exactly “φ = 1”. The Δyt−i terms mop up serial correlation so ε is white noise.
Formula. Three variants by deterministics: none (α = β = 0), drift (β = 0), trend (both in). Large-sample 5% critical values for the t-ratio on γ: −1.95 (none), −2.86 (drift), −3.41 (trend) — MacKinnon. NOT the t-table’s −1.65!
Typical values. Price levels and FX rates: fail to reject. Returns and growth rates: reject overwhelmingly (t-ratios of −10 or beyond). Lag length p: information criteria or Schwert’s rule 4(T/100)1/4.
At the extremes. Too few δ lags → leftover serial correlation → wrong size. Too many → power bleeds away. Wrong deterministics is the killer: test a trending series without the trend term and you will “find” a unit root every time (Platform 5 proves it by Monte Carlo).
Estimation problems. Under H₀ the t-ratio follows the Dickey–Fuller distribution, not Student’s t; power is dismal near φ = 1 (0.97 vs 1.00 is nearly invisible at n = 100); one structural break makes ADF non-reject even when each regime is stationary (Perron — see the clinic).
▾ detailsKPSSKPSS vs ADF — opposite nulls
ADF assumes a unit root until proven stationary; KPSS assumes stationarity until proven otherwise. Use both.
Meaning. ADF: H₀ = unit root. KPSS: H₀ = stationarity. Running both is confirmatory analysis — each test cross-examines the other’s null, which matters because “failing to reject” is weak evidence on its own.
Formula. KPSS = T−2ΣtSt²/σ̂²LR, where St is the partial sum of demeaned (or detrended) data and σ̂²LR a long-run variance. 5% critical values: 0.463 (level version), 0.146 (trend version) — reject for LARGE values.
Typical values / the 2×2.

Returns land in the top-left; price levels in the bottom-right.
At the extremes. “Both reject” is not a contradiction — it is the data telling you the I(0)/I(1) dichotomy itself is wrong for this series. “Neither rejects” happens constantly with n < 100.
Estimation problems. KPSS needs a bandwidth choice for σ̂²LR and the verdict can flip with it; both tests are size-distorted by large negative MA components (exactly what over-differencing creates); neither test is reliable straight after a structural break.

For an AR(1), a 1-unit shock still weighs φh after h periods, so its half-life is ln(½)/ln|φ|. Drag φ all the way to 1.00 — the random walk — and watch memory become permanent. Then inject shocks into a live series and watch them melt (or not).

Unit roots & the spurious regression disaster

Granger & Newbold (1974): regress one random walk on another, completely independent random walk, and the t-test screams “significant relationship!” far more often than the 5% it promises. This single Monte Carlo is why we difference prices (or test for cointegration) before regressing. Each run: 200 fresh regressions, every t-statistic computed by OLS.

200
What to notice. Press ▶ to run. A correct 5% test should reject H₀: β = 0 in roughly 10 of the 200 regressions…
🧪 Try this.
  1. Levels, n = 200: expect roughly 80% rejections and a healthy-looking average R² — for two series that have NOTHING to do with each other.
  2. Same walks, first-differenced: rejections collapse to ≈ 5% and R² to ≈ 0. Differencing restores honesty.
  3. Levels with n = 400: MORE data makes it WORSE — the spurious t-statistic grows like √n. You cannot out-sample a unit root.
  4. Think: “Bitcoin and the number of Greggs outlets both trended up 2015–2021, R² = 0.9”. Which platform-4 row is that headline?

ADF testing bench — size, power, and getting the deterministics wrong

Pick a true data-generating process, a sample size and an ADF specification, then run 300 Monte-Carlo replications and see how often the test rejects H₀: unit root at 5%. Honesty box: each ADF here is computed properly — OLS of Δyt on yt−1 (plus a constant and/or trend according to the spec) and one augmentation lag Δyt−1; the t-ratio on γ is compared with the standard large-sample MacKinnon 5% critical values, hard-coded as −1.95 (none), −2.86 (drift), −3.41 (trend). One augmentation lag is exactly right for these AR(1)-type DGPs.

300
What to notice. Press ▶ to run. Remember the logic: H₀ is the unit root, so under a true random walk the rejection rate measures the test’s SIZE (should be ≈ 5%), and under a stationary DGP it measures POWER (the higher the better).
🧪 Try this.
  1. Pure random walk, drift spec, n = 300. H₀ is TRUE here, so a healthy test rejects ≈ 5% of the time — that is its size, and the Dickey–Fuller critical value −2.86 is doing its job.
  2. Power: AR(0.8) at n = 300 rejects nearly always — but the near-unit-root AR(0.97) at n = 100 rejects well under half the time. With short samples you simply cannot tell 0.97 from 1.00.
  3. Misspecification: trend-stationary data tested with the drift spec essentially NEVER rejects — the omitted trend hides inside yt−1 and masquerades as a unit root ; add the trend term and power comes flooding back.
  4. Disaster class: a random walk WITH drift tested with the “none” spec — watch the whole t-statistic histogram slide to the POSITIVE side, nowhere near the rejection region. The verdict is “unit root”, but for completely wrong reasons: always match the deterministics to the data.

⚠️ Problems clinic

Ten classic ways ARMA and unit-root work goes wrong on real financial data. Click a card for symptoms, detection and the fix — most have a live demo.

▾ openOver-differencing
Differencing a series that was already stationary — “just to be safe”.
What it is. Taking Δy when y is already I(0). You have not made the data safer; you have injected a unit root into the MA side: Δyt = εt − εt−1 has θ = −1, exactly on the non-invertibility boundary.
How it bites. A common rookie move is differencing returns. The resulting series is noisier (variance roughly doubles), forecasts are strictly worse, and the ML estimate of θ slams into −1 with convergence warnings.
How to detect. The ACF of the over-differenced series shows one big NEGATIVE spike ≈ −0.5 at lag 1; the variance went UP after differencing (for a true unit root it goes down); θ̂₁ ≈ −1.
Remedy. Difference only when an ADF test fails to reject the unit root; compare variances before/after; if θ̂₁ ≈ −1, step back to the undifferenced data.
▾ openAIC overfitting vs BIC parsimony
AIC’s light penalty keeps buying parameters you don’t need.
What it is. AIC charges 2 per parameter, BIC charges ln(n). AIC is optimised for prediction error in large, honest worlds; in practice on noisy financial data it routinely picks ARMA(3,3) when the truth is ARMA(1,1) — you saw it happen in Platform 2.
How it bites. An overfitted model wins in-sample and loses out-of-sample — for a trading desk that is the difference between backtest profits and live losses. Extra ARMA terms also destabilise long-horizon forecasts.
How to detect. AIC and BIC champions differ; the bigger model’s extra coefficients have huge standard errors; pseudo-out-of-sample RMSE (week 6) favours the smaller model.
Remedy. Prefer BIC at moderate n for forecasting; confirm with a hold-out sample; when in doubt, leave it out.
▾ openARMA redundancy / common factors
AR and MA parts that cancel: (1−0.7L)y = (1−0.7L)ε is just white noise in fancy dress.
What it is. If the AR and MA polynomials share a (near-)common root, infinitely many parameter pairs describe the same data — the model is unidentified.
How it bites. Auto-fitted ARMA(2,2)s are notorious: the optimiser fails to converge or returns φ̂ and θ̂ that are large, opposite-signed and individually “significant-looking”, yet the model forecasts exactly like white noise. Fragile, embarrassing, common.
How to detect. Roots of the AR and MA polynomials nearly equal; enormous standard errors on offsetting coefficients; AIC/BIC barely improve over the (p−1, q−1) model.
Remedy. Cut both orders by one and refit. If the fit barely worsens, the factors were redundant — keep the smaller model.
▾ openStructural breaks mimicking long memory
One mean-shift makes a short-memory series look like φ ≈ 1.
What it is. A level shift (2008, COVID, a policy regime change) inflates sample autocorrelations at ALL lags: the ACF decays painfully slowly even though within each regime memory is short.
How it bites. You estimate φ̂ = 0.97, conclude “highly persistent”, and your forecasts revert to a long-run mean that is an average of two regimes — a number the process will never visit again.
How to detect. PLOT THE SERIES (always first); Chow or CUSUM tests; estimate the ACF on subsamples — if pre- and post-2008 ACFs look completely different, suspect a break, not memory.
Remedy. Model the break (dummy variable or split samples) or estimate on a rolling window; do not difference a broken-mean series into oblivion.
▾ openSeasonality contaminating the ACF
Spikes at lags 12, 24 (monthly) or 4, 8 (quarterly) that ARMA(p,q) cannot soak up.
What it is. Periodic structure — dividend months, quarterly earnings, year-end window dressing, January effects — puts repeating spikes into the correlogram at seasonal lags.
How it bites. You fit ARMA(1,1), the Ljung–Box at lag 12 still rejects, so you add lags 2, 3, 4… chasing a pattern that lives at lag 12. The model bloats and still fails diagnostics.
How to detect. ACF spikes at exact multiples of the seasonal period; monthly dummies jointly significant when added.
Remedy. Seasonal differencing (Δ₁₂), SARMA terms, or seasonal dummy variables — treat the season explicitly instead of brute-forcing more ordinary lags.
▾ openForecasting random walks (the EMH tie-in)
If prices are a random walk, the best forecast of tomorrow is… today.
What it is. Fitting ARMA to price LEVELS manufactures apparent predictability: φ̂ ≈ 0.99 with a tiny standard error looks like a money machine, but it is just the unit root.
How it bites. The “model” predicts tomorrow’s price ≈ today’s price — its impressive fit is 100% hindsight and 0% foresight. After transaction costs, trading it loses money. This is the efficient-markets hypothesis in econometric clothing: in informationally efficient markets, returns (not prices) should be near white noise.
How to detect. Compare forecast RMSE against the naive no-change forecast (it will not beat it — week 6’s tournament proves this); ADF cannot reject the unit root; out-of-sample “predictions” are just lagged prices.
Remedy. Model returns, not prices. Treat any claimed price-level predictability as a red flag for spurious regression or look-ahead bias, not as alpha.
▾ openADF and KPSS disagree — what now?
One test says unit root, the other says stationary. Both followed the rules.
What it is. ADF (H₀: unit root) and KPSS (H₀: stationary) are designed as a cross-examination — see the 2×2 table in the concept card above. The awkward cells: both reject, or neither rejects.
How it bites. A pairs-trading desk tests a price spread: ADF cannot reject the unit root, KPSS rejects stationarity → genuinely non-stationary, the “mean reversion” is a mirage. Worse is when both reject: the spread is neither I(0) nor I(1) — a regime break or near-fractional persistence — and a strategy built on either label bleeds.
How to detect. The disagreement IS the detection. Check which cell you are in, then ask why: n < 100 (neither rejects = low power, not evidence); a visible break in the plot (both tests distorted); a strongly negative MA component from over-differencing (size distortions in both).
Remedy. A practical workflow: (1) plot the series and its ACF first; (2) if neither rejects, get more data or accept agnosticism — do NOT cherry-pick the test that suits your story; (3) if both reject, test for breaks (Perron card below) before reaching for exotic long-memory models; (4) difference once, retest both, and check you have not landed on θ ≈ −1 (over-differencing card above); (5) report both tests — one p-value is advocacy, two is analysis.
▾ openA structural break masquerading as a unit root (Perron)
One regime shift and ADF waves the “random walk” flag — even though each regime is calmly stationary.
What it is. Perron (1989): if a series is stationary around a mean (or trend) that jumps ONCE — an oil shock, 2008, Brexit — the standard ADF test loses almost all power and fails to reject the unit root. The break makes the series look infinitely persistent to a test that assumes no break. (Its sibling card above covers the same break faking a high φ̂; this card is about the unit-root test being fooled.)
How it bites. Perron re-examined the classic Nelson–Plosser macro series that “everyone knew” were unit roots and found most were trend-stationary with one 1929/1973 break. Practically: you conclude I(1), difference, and forecast with no mean reversion — when the truth is a stationary process that will revert within the current regime. Risk models inherit a permanent-shock world that does not exist.
How to detect. PLOT THE SERIES — the demo below makes it obvious; subsample ADF tests (each regime alone often rejects comfortably); breakpoint-robust unit-root tests (Zivot–Andrews, Perron) that allow a break under both hypotheses; a Chow test at the suspected date.
Remedy. Model the break explicitly — a level-shift dummy plus an AR around it — or use break-robust unit-root tests before differencing. Differencing a broken-mean stationary series both over-differences AND smears the break into one giant outlier.
▾ openAdditive outliers wrecking the ACF
One fat-finger print or flash-crash return and every ρ̂k collapses towards zero.
What it is. An additive outlier — a data error, a flash crash, one COVID day — enters the ACF formula twice: it barely moves the cross-products at most lags but explodes the variance ĉ₀ in the denominator. Result: every sample autocorrelation is attenuated towards 0, and genuine dynamics vanish from the correlogram.
How it bites. A series with solid AR(1) persistence (φ = 0.7) plus ONE 12σ outlier shows an ACF that looks like white noise — you fit ARMA(0,0), your forecasts ignore real momentum, and a single bad tick chose your model. The reverse also happens: two outliers a fixed distance apart manufacture a fake spike at that lag.
How to detect. Plot the series before any correlogram (a 12σ point is unmissable); compare the raw ACF with the ACF of winsorised or rank-transformed data — if they disagree violently, outliers are driving the raw one; outlier diagnostics in ARMA software (tso, intervention dummies).
Remedy. Verify the data point first (is it real?); winsorise or dummy genuine one-off events; prefer robust autocorrelation estimates when screening many series; never let one observation pick the model — refit without it and see if the order changes.
▾ openDeterministic vs stochastic trend — the wrong cure
Trend-stationary ⇒ detrend. Unit root ⇒ difference. Swap the cures and you create new pathologies.
What it is. Two ways a series can trend: around a deterministic line (yt = a + bt + stationary noise → remove by regression on t) or via a stochastic trend (random walk with drift → remove by differencing). They can look near-identical in a plot, but each demands its own cure.
How it bites. Cure A on disease B: differencing a trend-stationary series over-differences — Δy has an MA root at −1, noisier forecasts, θ̂ pinned at the boundary. Cure B on disease A: detrending a random walk leaves residuals that are still I(1) around the fitted line; their ACF shows huge, slowly-swinging pseudo-cycles (Nelson–Kang) — researchers have “discovered” business cycles that were nothing but detrended random walks.
How to detect. ADF with trend vs KPSS trend version (the confirmatory 2×2, trend edition); after treating, inspect the result — a −0.5 ACF spike at lag 1 says you over-differenced, slow majestic ACF waves say you under-differenced; Platform 5’s trend-stationary DGP shows how the wrong spec answers the wrong question.
Remedy. Decide the trend type BEFORE removing it: test with trend-inclusive specifications; when genuinely unsure, economists usually difference (the cost of mild over-differencing is smaller than spurious-cycle inference); never run cycle/correlation analysis on OLS-detrended levels without checking the residuals are actually I(0).
