OLS picks the line that minimises the residual sum of squares — this week you race it by hand. Learn the eight numbers of the CAPM regression in the cards, then work four platforms: fit your own line, watch the sampling distribution of β̂ build up draw by draw, run a futures hedging desk, and chase a time-varying beta with rolling windows. 📚 Parameters & concepts

Click a card to expand. The regression is ri − rf = α + β·(rm − rf) + ε.

α — intercept (Jensen's alpha)
Return earned with the market switched off — "skill", allegedly.

Meaning: the average excess return left over after paying for market exposure. The single most fought-over number in fund management.

Formula: α̂ = ȳ − β̂·x̄ in the CAPM regression on excess returns. CAPM theory says α = 0.

Typical values: for most stocks and funds, statistically zero. A persistent monthly α̂ of even 0.2–0.3% (≈3%/yr) before costs is exceptional; after fees, the average mutual fund's α is slightly negative.

At the extremes: large positive α with low t — luck; large α with high t — check the data before celebrating (survivorship? stale prices? wrong rf?).

Estimation problems: SE(α̂) is huge relative to plausible true alphas — you need decades of data to "prove" skill; and using raw instead of excess returns mechanically fakes α (see clinic).

β — slope (market beta)
How many % the stock moves when the market moves 1%.

Meaning: systematic risk exposure — the part of the stock's movement you can't diversify away.

Formula: β̂ = Cov(x,y)/Var(x) = Σ(x−x̄)(y−ȳ)/Σ(x−x̄)².

Typical values: utilities/staples 0.3–0.7; the average stock 1 by construction; banks and tech 1.2–1.8; levered crypto-linked names 2+.

At the extremes: β = 0 — market-neutral (cash, some hedge funds); β < 0 — a genuine hedge (rare: gold miners sometimes, long-vol strategies); β = 1 — moves one-for-one (test H₀: β = 1, not just β = 0!).

Estimation problems: unstable over time (see Platform 4), attenuated by thin trading, hijacked by single outliers, and sensitive to the return frequency you choose.

σε — residual volatility
Idiosyncratic risk: the stock's own private drama.

Meaning: the standard deviation of ε — moves the market cannot explain (earnings surprises, CEO tweets).

Formula: s = √(RSS/(n−2)); total variance splits as β²σ²m + σ²ε.

Typical values: for monthly stock returns, σε ≈ 4–10% — usually BIGGER than the systematic part for a single stock; near zero for index trackers.

At the extremes: σε → 0 ⇒ R² → 1 (the stock IS the market); huge σε ⇒ scatter is a shapeless cloud and β̂ is poorly determined.

Estimation problems: it sits in the numerator of every SE — heteroscedastic ε (vol clustering) makes the usual s wrong, biasing all your t-stats.

SE(β̂) — standard error of beta
How much β̂ would wobble across alternative samples.

Meaning: the sampling-noise yardstick: the sd of the sampling distribution of β̂ (watch it materialise on Platform 2).

Formula: SE(β̂) = s/√Σ(x−x̄)² ≈ σε/(σx√n).

Typical values: with 60 monthly observations a stock beta carries SE ≈ 0.15–0.3 — so "β = 1.2" really means "somewhere between 0.7 and 1.7". Humbling.

At the extremes: quadruple n to halve SE; double the noise σε to double it; a market that never moves (tiny σx) makes β unidentifiable.

Estimation problems: the formula assumes homoscedastic, uncorrelated errors — both routinely false in finance; robust (White/Newey–West, Week 4) versions are bigger and more honest.

t-stat — estimate ÷ its SE
How many standard errors the estimate sits from the hypothesised value.

Meaning: the signal-to-noise ratio of a coefficient; |t| > ~2 means "hard to explain by sampling luck alone".

Formula: t = (β̂ − β₀)/SE(β̂) ~ t(n−2) under H₀. β₀ = 0 for "is there any market exposure?", β₀ = 1 for "does it move one-for-one?".

Typical values: betas are nearly always significant vs 0 (t of 5–20); alphas almost never are (t of 0–1.5).

At the extremes: t = 1.96 ⇔ p = 0.05 with large n; in big samples economically tiny effects become "significant" — read the size of β̂, not just its stars.

Estimation problems: garbage SEs ⇒ garbage t-stats — autocorrelation and heteroscedasticity typically inflate |t| and manufacture false discoveries.

R² — goodness of fit
The share of the stock's variance the market explains.

Meaning: in the CAPM context, the systematic share of total risk. Also the squared correlation between x and y in the bivariate case.

Formula: R² = 1 − RSS/TSS = β̂²Σ(x−x̄)²/Σ(y−ȳ)².

Typical values: single stocks vs the index: 0.1–0.4; diversified portfolios: 0.8+; index trackers: 0.99+. A LOW R² is normal and fine for a single stock.

At the extremes: R² ≈ 1 in a "returns" regression should ring alarm bells — you probably regressed price LEVELS (see clinic); R² ≈ 0 with significant β is perfectly possible in big samples.

Estimation problems: R² says nothing about whether the MODEL is right, and maximising it invites junk regressors (next week's topic).

n — sample size
Observations: the only free lunch — except it isn't.

Meaning: number of return periods in the regression window.

Formula: SE ∝ 1/√n; df = n − 2 for the t-tests.

Typical values: the industry-standard beta uses 60 monthly or 252 daily observations.

At the extremes: n = 20 — SEs so wide the estimate is decorative; n = 2500 (10y daily) — precise estimate of an average that may mix several different regimes.

Estimation problems: the n vs stability trade-off — long windows shrink sampling noise but smear over genuine changes in β (Platform 4 makes you feel this).

h* — optimal hedge ratio
Futures per unit of spot that minimises P&L variance.

Meaning: how many futures contracts to short per unit of spot exposure. It is exactly an OLS slope: regress spot returns on futures returns.

Formula: h* = Cov(Δs, Δf)/Var(Δf); hedged variance = Var(Δs)·(1 − ρ²).

Typical values: hedging an equity portfolio with index futures: h* ≈ portfolio beta; cross-hedges (jet fuel with crude futures) h* ≈ 0.5–0.9 with sizeable basis risk.

At the extremes: ρ = 1 ⇒ perfect hedge, zero residual risk; ρ = 0 ⇒ h* = 0, hedging adds pure noise; h = 1 ("naive full hedge") is only optimal if Cov = Var.

Estimation problems: estimated from history — if the spot–futures relationship shifts (basis blow-outs, 2020 oil), yesterday's h* mis-hedges today; time-varying versions need GARCH (Week 7).

⚠️ Problems clinic

Six classic ways CAPM regressions and hedge ratios go wrong on a real desk.

▸ 1 · Beta instability: yesterday's beta, today's risk

What it is: β is treated as a constant but drifts with leverage, business mix and market regime. The number you estimated is a backward-looking average.

How it bites: banks went into 2008 with "β ≈ 0.9" estimated on calm years, then traded like β ≈ 1.8 through the crisis — hedges sized on the old number were half the size they needed to be.

How to detect: rolling-window β̂ (Platform 4); Chow test for a break at a known date; recursive estimates drifting outside their bands (Week 4).

Remedy: shorter/exponentially-weighted windows, shrink extreme betas toward 1 (Vasicek/Bloomberg adjustment: 0.67·β̂ + 0.33·1), or model the time variation explicitly.

▸ 2 · Price levels instead of returns: the R² = 0.99 trap

What it is: regressing one trending price LEVEL on another. Both wander upward, so the regression "fits" superbly — even when the assets are completely unrelated. The spurious regression problem.

How it bites: a student regresses Apple's price on the S&P level, gets R² = 0.98 and t = 40, and concludes Apple is "almost perfectly explained by the market". Re-done in returns, R² is 0.3.

How to detect: R² suspiciously near 1 plus a Durbin–Watson statistic near 0 is the classic signature; ask yourself "did I difference the data?"

Remedy: regress RETURNS on RETURNS. Levels regressions are only legitimate for cointegrated pairs (Year 3 material).

▸ 3 · Thin trading: the incredible shrinking beta

What it is: illiquid stocks don't trade every period, so quoted prices are stale. Today's market move shows up in the stock's measured return tomorrow — contemporaneous covariance is understated and β̂ is biased toward zero (attenuation).

How it bites: small-cap and frontier-market funds look "low risk" (β̂ ≈ 0.4) purely because of stale pricing; risk budgets and hedge sizes set on these betas are too small.

How to detect: regress the stock's return on the market's current AND lagged returns — significant lagged coefficients betray staleness; β̂ rises when you use weekly instead of daily returns.

Remedy: Dimson beta (sum of coefficients on lags −1, 0, +1), or estimate at lower frequency where staleness washes out.

▸ 4 · One outlier owns the slope

What it is: OLS minimises SQUARED errors, so a single extreme (x, y) pair — especially one far out in x — exerts enormous leverage on β̂.

How it bites: estimate a beta over a window containing one crash day with a data error (or a genuine freak event) and β̂ can halve, double, or change sign. Your hedge follows it.

How to detect: ALWAYS plot the scatter; leverage statistics/Cook's distance; re-estimate dropping the largest |x| observation and compare.

Remedy: verify the data point first (fact, not noise?). If it's an error, fix it; if real, report both estimates — and consider robust regression for sizing decisions.

▸ 5 · "Just hedge 1-for-1": the h = 1 fallacy

What it is: assuming the right hedge is always one futures contract per unit of spot. h = 1 is only optimal when Cov(Δs,Δf) = Var(Δf) — i.e. a regression slope of exactly 1.

How it bites: cross-hedges (airline fuel with Brent, a stock portfolio with index futures when portfolio β = 1.4) mis-hedge badly at h = 1: you're left with either residual exposure or, worse, added futures risk.

How to detect: estimate the regression Δs = a + h·Δf + u. If the slope's CI excludes 1, the naive hedge is statistically wrong; the hedging-desk platform shows how much variance it costs.

Remedy: use h* = Cov/Var (the OLS slope), re-estimate it regularly, and remember the hedge quality ceiling is ρ² — no ratio fixes a bad proxy.

▸ 6 · Jensen's alpha on raw returns: free fake skill

What it is: the CAPM is a statement about excess returns. Regressing raw returns on raw market returns gives an intercept of α + (1−β)·r̄f — not alpha.

How it bites: a low-beta fund (β = 0.5) in a 4% interest-rate world picks up a fake "alpha" of (1−0.5)·4% = 2%/yr from the arithmetic alone. Marketing departments adore this error.

How to detect: check the data pipeline — was rf subtracted from BOTH sides? Suspect any reported alpha that shrinks when rates fall.

Remedy: build excess returns first (r − rf,monthly, mind the annual→monthly conversion: divide the annual % by 12), then regress.
