MANG2074 · Financial Econometrics 1Week 3 — Multiple Regression & F-tests
← All topics
Add more regressors and R² always goes up — even when the “factors” are pure noise. This week you stuff a kitchen-sink regression with junk, dissect the F-statistic, watch multicollinearity stretch your estimates along a ridge, and build model specifications against a known truth. Then the clinic, the “Significant or Spurious?” game, and the code.

📚 Parameters & concepts

Click a card to expand. The model is y = β₀ + β₁x₁ + … + βkxk + u.

k — number of regressors
How many explanatory variables you put in (excluding the constant).

Meaning: the dimensionality of your model. Each regressor spends one degree of freedom of your data.

Formula: residual df = n − k − 1; every t-test and the F-test reference it.

Typical values: asset-pricing models: 1 (CAPM) to 5–6 (Fama–French + momentum); corporate-finance panels: 5–15 controls.

At the extremes: k = 0 — just the mean; k → n−1 — perfect "fit" with zero residual df, explaining nothing about the future; the academic "factor zoo" now exceeds 400 published factors.

Estimation problems: every extra regressor inflates the variance of the others (especially if correlated) and invites data mining — see the clinic.

R² — coefficient of determination
Share of variance "explained" — and a number that can only go up.

Meaning: the fraction of Var(y) reproduced by the fitted values.

Formula: R² = 1 − RSS/TSS. Mechanically non-decreasing in k.

Typical values: daily stock returns on factors: 0.1–0.4; macro quarterly regressions: 0.6–0.9; anything near 1 with trending series = spurious (Week 2 clinic).

At the extremes: R² = 1 ⇔ y is an exact linear combination of the regressors (or you included y itself by accident — it happens); R² = 0 ⇔ slopes all zero.

Estimation problems: useless for comparing models with different k or different y transformations; maximising it selects junk.

Adjusted R² — R̄²
R² with a penalty for wasted degrees of freedom.

Meaning: rises only if a new regressor pulls its weight (|t| > 1, roughly).

Formula: R̄² = 1 − (RSS/(n−k−1))/(TSS/(n−1)).

Typical values: a whisker below R²; the gap widens as k grows relative to n.

At the extremes: can go NEGATIVE — your model fits worse than a horizontal line once df costs are billed; equals R² only when k = 0.

Estimation problems: a soft penalty — it still favours slightly-overfit models; AIC/BIC punish harder.

F — joint test statistic
"Do these m restrictions cost a suspicious amount of fit?"

Meaning: compares the restricted model's RSS with the unrestricted one's — the price of imposing H₀, per restriction, in units of residual variance.

Formula: F = [(RRSS − URSS)/m] ÷ [URSS/(n−k−1)] ~ F(m, n−k−1) under H₀.

Typical values: 5% critical values: F(1,100) ≈ 3.94, F(5,100) ≈ 2.31, F(10,100) ≈ 1.93 — more restrictions ⇒ lower bar per restriction.

At the extremes: F < 1 — the restrictions barely cost anything; F with m = 1 equals t² for the same hypothesis; the "overall" F tests all slopes = 0 at once.

Estimation problems: assumes homoscedastic, uncorrelated errors — with financial data use robust (Wald) versions or the conclusion can be pure artefact.

m — number of restrictions
How many separate "=" signs your hypothesis contains.

Meaning: the count of independent constraints under test — the numerator df of the F.

Formula: H₀: β₂ = β₃ = 0 is m = 2; H₀: β₂ + β₃ = 1 is m = 1 (one equation, two coefficients!).

Typical values: 1 (a t-test in disguise) to k (the overall F-test).

At the extremes: the same total RSS gap spread over more restrictions gives a SMALLER F — a genuine effect can hide inside a big sloppy joint test.

Estimation problems: counting m wrong is the classic exam error: "β₂ = β₃ = 0" (m=2) vs "β₂ = β₃" (m=1) vs "β₂ + β₃ = 0" (m=1, different meaning again).

VIF — variance inflation factor
How much multicollinearity inflates a coefficient's variance.

Meaning: the multiplier on Var(β̂ⱼ) caused by xⱼ being predictable from the other regressors.

Formula: VIFⱼ = 1/(1−R²ⱼ), R²ⱼ from regressing xⱼ on all the other x's. SE inflates by √VIF.

Typical values: below 5 comfortable; 10+ is the standard alarm; size and book-to-market factors typically 1.5–3.

At the extremes: ρ = 0.95 between two regressors ⇒ VIF ≈ 10.3; ρ = 0.99 ⇒ VIF = 50.3; perfect collinearity ⇒ VIF = ∞ and (X′X) is singular — software refuses or silently drops a column.

Estimation problems: high VIF ⇒ huge SEs, sign flips between samples, but the model's FORECASTS and the SUM of the twin coefficients stay fine — see Platform 3.

t vs F — one knife, one net
t tests a single coefficient; F tests a team of them jointly.

Meaning: t answers "does xⱼ matter, given everything else?"; F answers "does this GROUP matter at all?". They can disagree — legitimately.

Formula: for m = 1, F = t²; otherwise F pools the joint evidence including the covariances between estimates.

Typical values: under multicollinearity: all individual |t| < 2 yet joint F strongly significant — the group matters, the data just can't say which member.

At the extremes: the reverse (one big t, insignificant joint F) signals m is too big — significance diluted across irrelevant restrictions.

Estimation problems: dropping variables one-by-one on t-ratios (general-to-specific) can dismantle a jointly significant block — always F-test the block before deleting it.

AIC / BIC — information criteria
Fit minus a fine for complexity; lower is better.

Meaning: model-selection scores trading off RSS against the number of parameters — usable across non-nested models, unlike F-tests.

Formula: AIC = n·ln(RSS/n) + 2K; BIC = n·ln(RSS/n) + K·ln(n), K = parameters incl. constant.

Typical values: only DIFFERENCES matter; the absolute level is meaningless. ΔAIC > 2 between models is conventionally "real".

At the extremes: ln(n) > 2 once n ≥ 8, so BIC penalises harder and picks SMALLER models than AIC, essentially always; AIC tends to overselect.

Estimation problems: comparing AICs across different y's (levels vs logs) is meaningless; both criteria still assume you brought the true model to the party.

⚠️ Problems clinic

Six ways multiple regressions mislead — and how to catch them.

▸ 1 · Omitted-variable bias: the formula that tells you WHICH WAY you're wrong

What it is: leaving out a relevant variable that's correlated with an included one. The included variable picks up its friend's work: plim β̂₁ = β₁ + β₂·δ, where δ is the slope from regressing the omitted x₂ on the included x₁.

How it bites: estimating a "value effect" while omitting size (correlated with value) — your value premium is part size premium. Sign rule: bias = (sign of β₂) × (sign of Corr) — you can predict the direction before running anything.

How to detect: coefficients that move a lot when you add controls; theory telling you a confounder exists; comparing nested specifications (Platform 4).

Remedy: include the variable (best), find a proxy, or use instruments/panel methods (later weeks). NO amount of extra data fixes it — it's bias, not noise.

▸ 2 · Irrelevant variables: bias-free but never free

What it is: including regressors whose true coefficient is zero. Estimates stay unbiased — but every SE in the model inflates, especially when the junk correlates with real regressors.

How it bites: a "comprehensive" 15-control regression where the variable you care about is now insignificant — not because the effect vanished but because you burned the precision. df also shrink: with n = 60 and k = 15 your t critical values quietly grow.

How to detect: adjusted R² falls when the variable enters; |t| < 1; AIC/BIC rise; block F-tests on groups of controls.

Remedy: theory-first selection, general-to-specific testing with F-tests on blocks (not one-at-a-time t fishing), information criteria as referee.

▸ 3 · Multicollinearity: significant together, invisible apart

What it is: regressors that move together. (X′X) becomes near-singular; individual coefficients are barely identified even though the model fits well.

How it bites: "size" and "liquidity" in the same regression (ρ ≈ 0.9): both t's collapse below 1, a referee asks you to drop "the insignificant ones", and you delete a genuinely priced factor. Sign flips across subsamples destroy credibility.

How to detect: the symptom triad — high R² + significant overall F + individually insignificant t's; VIF > 10; coefficient instability across subsamples (Platform 3's cloud).

Remedy: more data (the only true cure), combine the twins into one variable (e.g. an index), drop one ON THEORY GROUNDS and say so, or accept that you know the sum but not the split.

▸ 4 · Data mining & the factor zoo: torture the data until they confess

What it is: testing many candidate variables at the 5% level guarantees "discoveries": testing 20 pure-noise factors yields about one significant t by luck. Finance journals have published hundreds of return "factors"; most fail out-of-sample.

How it bites: your backtest tries 50 signals, three clear t > 2, you trade the three — and they immediately stop working, because they never worked. Multiple testing is THE silent killer of quant strategies.

How to detect: ask "how many things were tried?"; demand out-of-sample evidence; with N tests, expect N×5% false positives; Harvey–Liu–Zhu argue new factors should clear |t| > 3, not 2.

Remedy: hold-out samples, Bonferroni-style corrections (α/N), pre-registration of hypotheses, economic priors before statistical sifting.

▸ 5 · The dummy variable trap: perfect collinearity by construction

What it is: including a dummy for EVERY category plus the intercept. The dummies sum to 1 = the constant column — exact linear dependence, (X′X) singular.

How it bites: four quarter dummies + constant in a seasonality regression: R errors out or silently drops one; Python's patsy quietly picks a base category — and students then misread the coefficients, which are now differences from the base, not level effects.

How to detect: software complaints ("singular matrix", NA coefficients) or one dummy mysteriously missing from the output.

Remedy: use g−1 dummies with an intercept (coefficients = gaps to the base), or all g dummies with NO intercept (coefficients = category means). Pick one and interpret accordingly.

▸ 6 · R² worship: fit is not truth

What it is: treating R² as the score of the analysis. R² measures in-sample correlation with fitted values — not correctness, causality, or forecastability.

How it bites: in finance, GOOD models of daily returns have R² ≈ 0.01–0.05 — and that's tradeable! Meanwhile spurious levels regressions flaunt R² = 0.99. Selecting strategies by in-sample R² selects overfit ones.

How to detect: ask what R² SHOULD be for this problem; compare with adjusted R², AIC/BIC, and out-of-sample RMSE; be suspicious of fits that are too good.

Remedy: judge models on out-of-sample performance, economically sensible coefficients and surviving diagnostic tests (Week 4) — not on the prettiest in-sample number.
