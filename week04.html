The Gauss–Markov assumptions don’t break loudly — they break in the residual plots. This week you deliberately infect a regression with heteroscedasticity, autocorrelation, fat-tailed errors or the wrong functional form; benchmark OLS vs White (HC1/HC3) vs Newey–West standard errors by Monte-Carlo coverage; hunt structural breaks with rolling estimates, Chow and CUSUM; measure exactly how much autocorrelation makes your t-stats lie; plant outliers by hand and watch leverage steal your β̂; and train the diagnostic eye on residual curvature. Then the clinic, the Regression Doctor game, and the code.

📚 Parameters & concepts

Click a card to expand. Thirteen cards from the diagnostic toolkit — each one answers "can I trust this regression?"

BP / White LM — heteroscedasticity test
Does the error variance depend on the regressors?

Meaning: regress squared residuals û² on the regressors (Breusch–Pagan) or regressors + squares + cross-products (White). If they explain û², variance isn't constant.

Formula: LM = n·R²aux ~ χ²(p), p = regressors in the auxiliary regression (crit 5.99 for p = 2).

Typical values: with volatility-clustered financial returns, LM rejects almost always at daily frequency.

At the extremes: LM ≈ 0 — û² unpredictable (good); huge LM — variance strongly structured. White also reacts to misspecified functional form: a red light isn't a unique diagnosis.

Estimation problems: White's version with many regressors eats df (all the squares and cross terms); both tests need the mean equation to be right first.

DW d — Durbin–Watson
First-order autocorrelation of residuals; healthy ≈ 2.

Meaning: measures whether neighbouring residuals look alike. d ≈ 2(1−ρ̂): positive autocorrelation pushes d below 2, negative above.

Formula: d = Σ(ût − ût−1)² / Σût², bounded in [0, 4].

Typical values: d = 2 ⇒ ρ̂ = 0; d = 1 ⇒ ρ̂ ≈ 0.5; d = 0.4 ⇒ ρ̂ ≈ 0.8 (a screaming alarm); levels regressions of trending series often give d < 0.5 — the spurious-regression fingerprint.

At the extremes: d → 0 — residuals are basically a random walk; d → 4 — flip-flopping residuals (over-differenced data).

Estimation problems: only tests lag 1; invalid with lagged dependent variables (use BG instead); has an "inconclusive zone" in exact small-sample inference.

BG — Breusch–Godfrey, p lags
DW's bigger sibling: autocorrelation at any chosen lag depth.

Meaning: regress ût on the X's plus ût−1…ût−p; if the lags help, residuals remember their past.

Formula: LM = (n−p)·R²aux ~ χ²(p).

Typical values: p = 4 for quarterly data, 12 for monthly, 5–10 for daily — match p to plausible memory.

At the extremes: p too small misses seasonal autocorrelation; p too large wastes power. Valid even with lagged y's, unlike DW.

Estimation problems: rejecting tells you residuals correlate but not WHY — omitted dynamics, misspecification, or genuinely AR errors all trigger it; each remedy is different.

JB — Jarque–Bera on residuals
Are the residuals normal enough for exact t and F inference?

Meaning: tests residual skewness and excess kurtosis jointly against the normal benchmark (Week 1's test, now applied to û).

Formula: JB = n/6·(S² + K²/4) ~ χ²(2), crit 5.99.

Typical values: with daily financial data, JB on residuals rejects nearly always — fat tails survive the regression.

At the extremes: mild non-normality + large n ⇒ harmless (CLT rescues the t-stats); huge JB driven by a couple of outliers ⇒ investigate the observations, not the distribution.

Estimation problems: in small samples non-normality genuinely distorts t and F; JB itself is unreliable below n ≈ 100 (Week 1 clinic).

VIF — variance inflation factor
Multicollinearity meter: how redundant is each regressor?

Meaning: VIFⱼ = the factor by which Var(β̂ⱼ) is inflated because xⱼ is predictable from the other regressors (Week 3's lab, now part of the standard check-up).

Formula: VIFⱼ = 1/(1−R²ⱼ); SE multiplier = √VIF.

Typical values: < 5 fine; > 10 (R²ⱼ > 0.9) the conventional alarm.

At the extremes: VIF = 1 — orthogonal luxury; VIF = ∞ — exact collinearity (dummy trap), estimation impossible.

Estimation problems: it's a symptom meter, not a test — no p-value, and dropping a variable to "fix" VIF can create omitted-variable bias, which is worse.

RESET — Ramsey's functional form test
Is "linear" the right shape at all?

Meaning: add powers of the fitted values (ŷ², ŷ³) to the regression; if they're significant, the conditional mean isn't linear in your X's.

Formula: t-test on ŷ² (or F-test on ŷ², ŷ³ jointly) in y = Xβ + γ₂ŷ² + γ₃ŷ³ + u.

Typical values: healthy linear DGP ⇒ p ≈ U(0,1); quadratic truth fitted linear ⇒ p < 0.001 routinely.

At the extremes: RESET rejecting ⇒ your coefficients are BIASED — robust standard errors cannot save you; the model itself must change (logs, squares, interactions).

Estimation problems: it detects "some" misspecification without naming it; significant RESET + significant White often have the same single cause.

Chow F — structural break test
Did the coefficients change at a known date?

Meaning: split the sample at a candidate break date, fit both halves separately, and ask whether the improvement over one pooled fit is too big for chance.

Formula: F = [(RSSpooled − RSS₁ − RSS₂)/k] ÷ [(RSS₁ + RSS₂)/(n − 2k)] ~ F(k, n−2k), k = parameters per regime.

Typical values: tested at 2008, 2020, Brexit… financial relationships fail Chow tests depressingly often.

At the extremes: break too near the sample edge ⇒ one regime has too few observations; unknown break date ⇒ trying every date inflates size (use sup-F/Quandt–Andrews critical values, not standard F).

Estimation problems: assumes equal error variance across regimes — crises break that too; CUSUM-type tests complement it when the date is unknown.

HC vs HAC — robust standard errors
White fixes heteroscedasticity; Newey–West fixes that AND autocorrelation.

Meaning: keep the OLS β̂ but replace the naive variance formula with one that's valid under heteroscedasticity (HC0–HC3) or heteroscedasticity + autocorrelation (HAC / Newey–West).

Formula: V = (X′X)⁻¹·S·(X′X)⁻¹ with S = Σû²txtx′t (HC), plus Bartlett-weighted cross-lag terms wl = 1 − l/(L+1) up to lag L (HAC).

Typical values: rule-of-thumb HAC lag L ≈ 0.75·n1/3 (≈ 4 for n = 150); robust SEs in finance are typically 1.2–3× the naive ones.

At the extremes: L = 0 reduces HAC to HC; L too large makes the estimate noisy. Coefficients NEVER change — only the SEs.

Estimation problems: robust SEs fix inference, never bias — see the clinic's "robust SEs fix everything" fallacy; in small samples HC0 understates, prefer HC1/HC3.

hii & Cook's D — leverage & influence
Which single observations could hijack your β̂?

Meaning: leverage hii = xi′(X′X)⁻¹xi measures how unusual observation i's x is — how hard that point CAN pull the fitted line towards itself. Cook's distance combines leverage with the residual's size to measure how much β̂ actually MOVES if you delete observation i.

Formula: Di = ûi²·hii / [k·s²·(1−hii)²] — equivalently (ri²/k)·hii/(1−hii) with standardised residual ri. Leverages always sum to k.

Typical values: average leverage = k/n; alarms at hii > 2k/n (or 3k/n) and Cook's D > 4/n (the classic stricter cut is D > 1).

At the extremes: hii → 1 — the point dictates its own fitted value, so its residual is forced towards 0: influential points hide in residual plots by dragging the line to themselves. hii = k/n exactly for a point at the centre of the x's — even a huge y-outlier there barely moves the slope.

Estimation problems: masking — two outliers can hide each other in delete-one diagnostics; deleting inconvenient observations is misconduct, so investigate first (data error? a real crash?); if the point is genuine, robust regression (Huber weights, LAD) downweights it — robust SEs do nothing here.

HC0–HC3 — the White family
Four flavours of het-robust SE; only the small-sample correction differs.

Meaning: all four build the same sandwich V = (X′X)⁻¹S(X′X)⁻¹; they differ only in how each squared residual enters S — i.e. in the finite-sample correction.

Formula: HC0: ûi²; HC1: ûi²·n/(n−k); HC2: ûi²/(1−hii); HC3: ûi²/(1−hii)².

Typical values: asymptotically identical. At n ≈ 50, HC0 SEs run 10–20% too small; HC3 ≈ the jackknife and gives the best small-sample coverage (MacKinnon–White 1985). Stata's robust = HC1; in statsmodels you choose.

At the extremes: high-leverage observations get progressively MORE weight up the ladder (1−hii small ⇒ big inflation) — exactly the points where OLS residuals understate the true error. n → ∞: all four converge; n small + leverage: HC0 badly under-covers (the Coverage lab shows it).

Estimation problems: none of them fixes autocorrelation (use HAC), bias (fix the model), or within-group correlation (use cluster-robust SEs — a different animal).

L — HAC / Newey–West bandwidth
How many lags of residual autocovariance enter the SE — the bias–variance dial.

Meaning: Newey–West adds Bartlett-weighted cross-lag terms up to lag L into the sandwich. L decides how much autocorrelation you insure against.

Formula: rule of thumb L = ⌊4(n/100)2/9⌋ — so 4 lags at n = 100, 5 at n = 500, 6 at n = 1000. Weights wl = 1 − l/(L+1) shrink with the lag and keep the variance estimate positive semi-definite.

Typical values: 4–8 for most financial regressions; with k-period overlapping returns you need L ≥ k−1 by construction (clinic card 2).

At the extremes: L = 0 collapses NW to plain White — zero protection against autocorrelation. L too large: the SE estimate itself becomes noisy (you're averaging ever-noisier autocovariances), so coverage can get WORSE again. L must grow with n, but slowly (∝ n2/9), for consistency.

Estimation problems: too few lags ⇒ leftover autocorrelation ⇒ still over-rejecting; data-driven (Andrews) bandwidths help; even with the right L, HAC under-covers in small samples — it is consistent, not unbiased.

RESET mechanics — power & blind spots
What the ŷ², ŷ³ trick can and cannot detect.

Meaning: mechanically, RESET is just an F-test on added powers of the fitted values: under a correct linear spec, ŷ² and ŷ³ are functions of X with no extra explanatory power; under a wrong shape they proxy the missing curvature.

Formula: fit y = Xβ + γ₂ŷ² + γ₃ŷ³ + u; F = [(RSSr − RSSu)/2] ÷ [RSSu/(n−k−2)] ~ F(2, n−k−2).

Typical values: quadratic truth fitted linear at n = 100 ⇒ rejection essentially every sample; linear truth ⇒ rejects ≈5% of samples (the test's size) — the Functional-form lab lets you count both.

At the extremes: strong power against wrong transformations of YOUR x's (levels vs logs, missing squares); zero power against an omitted variable orthogonal to X — ŷ powers are built from X, so they cannot proxy something unrelated to X. A passing RESET never certifies the model complete.

Estimation problems: rejection says "shape wrong", not which shape; under heteroscedasticity use a robust-SE version of the F-test; piling on ŷ⁴, ŷ⁵… mostly burns power.

ln y — log transformations
When logging the data is the cure: skewed levels, multiplicative errors, elasticities.

Meaning: when y is positive and right-skewed (prices, market caps, volumes, salaries) and shocks are proportional ("±2% days", not "±£2 days"), errors are multiplicative — additive on the LOG scale. Logging makes them closer to normal and homoscedastic, and turns slopes into (semi-)elasticities.

Formula: ln y = a + b·ln x + ε ⇒ b = elasticity (%Δy per 1%Δx); ln y = a + b·x ⇒ 100·b ≈ %Δy per unit of x. Log returns Δln Pt are Week 1's standard object.

Typical values: empirical finance lives in logs — log size, log book-to-market, log price regressions; gravity-style elasticities typically 0.5–1.5.

At the extremes: y ≤ 0 cannot be logged (model returns, not "log of a loss"); values near 0 explode after logging and dominate the fit; if the truth is ADDITIVE, logging induces heteroscedasticity — a transformation is a hypothesis, so check residual plots both ways.

Estimation problems: retransformation bias — forecasting levels as exp(fitted) understates E[y] (need the ×exp(σ̂²/2) correction under normality); log(y+1) hacks distort elasticities near zero; log-model and level-model R²'s are NOT comparable (different dependent variables).

⚠️ Problems clinic

Ten diagnostic disasters from real empirical finance.

▸ 1 · Heteroscedasticity in volatility-clustered returns

What it is: financial error variance moves in regimes — calm spells, crisis spells. Any regression on daily returns inherits this: Var(ut) is a process, not a constant.

How it bites: event studies and CAPM regressions over windows containing a crisis get standard errors that average calm and storm — understating uncertainty exactly when observations are wildest, so "significant" announcements effects appear out of noise.

How to detect: White/BP on residuals; plot û² over time (clusters are visible to the naked eye); ARCH-LM test (Week 7 formalises it).

Remedy: White/HC SEs at minimum; better, model the variance with GARCH or weight observations (WLS); never quote naive SEs for daily-return regressions.

▸ 2 · Overlapping returns: autocorrelation you built yourself

What it is: using rolling k-period returns (e.g. 20-day returns computed daily) makes consecutive observations share k−1 days of data — mechanically autocorrelated errors with ACF declining linearly to lag k−1, even if daily returns are iid.

How it bites: long-horizon "return predictability" regressions (dividend yield predicting 5-year returns) have famously inflated t-stats; entire literatures shrank once HAC/overlap corrections were applied.

How to detect: you KNOW it's there by construction — check the residual ACF: triangular decay out to lag k−1 is the signature.

Remedy: Newey–West with L ≥ k−1 lags, use non-overlapping periods (fewer but honest observations), or Hansen–Hodrick SEs.

▸ 3 · Non-normality and small samples: when the CLT hasn't arrived yet

What it is: exact t and F distributions assume normal errors. With fat-tailed errors the CLT still rescues large samples, but in small ones (n < ~50) t-stats are genuinely mis-sized — especially with outlier-prone financial data.

How it bites: a hedge-fund due-diligence regression on 36 monthly observations with one −20% month: the t-distribution is the wrong yardstick, the outlier dominates β̂, and both Type I and Type II error rates are off.

How to detect: JB on residuals + a residual QQ-plot; ask "would deleting my biggest residual change the conclusion?"

Remedy: more data where possible; bootstrap inference; check robustness to outliers (dummy them or re-estimate without); with n large, mild non-normality is the ONE disease you may ignore.

▸ 4 · Functional-form misspecification: the disease robust SEs can't touch

What it is: fitting a line through a curve. The error term absorbs the systematic gap, so E[u|x] ≠ 0 — the one assumption whose failure BIASES β̂ rather than just its SE.

How it bites: bond price vs yield (convex!), option value vs moneyness, size effects that flatten out — fitted linear, the slope is an average of different local slopes and predicts badly everywhere; hedges sized on it are wrong in both tails.

How to detect: RESET (ŷ² significant); ∪/∩ patterns in residuals vs fitted; plot y against each x and LOOK.

Remedy: change the model — logs, squares, piecewise terms, interactions. White SEs on a misspecified mean = precisely estimated nonsense.

▸ 5 · Structural breaks: parameters with an expiry date

What it is: regression coefficients that changed during the sample — regime shifts from policy (QE), technology (HFT), or crises (2008, 2020). The "full-sample" estimate describes an average world that never existed.

How it bites: risk models calibrated across 2007–2009 averaged pre- and post-Lehman betas; pairs-trading relationships estimated across a break mean-revert to a mean that's gone. Forecasts from pre-break data fail silently.

How to detect: Chow F at suspected dates; CUSUM/recursive estimates when the date is unknown; rolling coefficients drifting (Platform 3); sup-F (Quandt–Andrews) for honest unknown-date inference.

Remedy: split the sample / dummy-interact the break; use post-break data for forecasting; regime-switching models (Week 9) when regimes recur.

▸ 6 · The "robust SEs fix everything" fallacy

What it is: the belief that slapping cov_type='HAC' on any regression makes it publishable. Robust SEs fix inference about an unbiased estimate. They do nothing about bias from omitted variables, wrong functional form, simultaneity, or structural breaks.

How it bites: a misspecified model with beautiful Newey–West t-stats is a confidently wrong model. The demo below: a quadratic DGP fitted linear — White SEs change the t-stat, but β̂ stays exactly as biased as before.

How to detect: ask which assumption failed. Errors' VARIANCE/CORRELATION wrong ⇒ robust SEs appropriate. Errors' MEAN related to x (RESET, OVB, endogeneity) ⇒ the estimate itself is broken.

Remedy: diagnose before prescribing (the game below trains exactly this); fix the mean equation first, then make the SEs robust as the final coat of paint.

▸ 7 · Mechanical fixes for structural problems: the omitted-variable edition

What it is: the cousin of fallacy 6, one level deeper. Robust SEs, HAC, even transformations are mechanical patches; if the pattern in your residuals is really caused by a variable you LEFT OUT that's correlated with x, every patch leaves the bias exactly where it was. The residual "disease" is a symptom of a missing organ, not bad plumbing.

How it bites: a momentum regression whose "autocorrelated errors" are an omitted risk factor moving slowly through time — Newey–West makes the t-stats beautiful and the coefficient stays contaminated; "heteroscedasticity" in a returns-on-size regression that's really an omitted volatility variable doing the fanning. Patch applied, conclusion still wrong.

How to detect: think economics before statistics — what SHOULD be in this model? Plot residuals against candidate omitted variables; coefficients that jump around across subsamples are a classic OVB fingerprint (the omitted variable's correlation with x shifts).

Remedy: add the variable; use fixed effects to absorb what you can't measure; instrumental variables when x is outright endogenous (Year 3 territory). Decision rule: residual pattern caused by how errors BEHAVE ⇒ robust SEs; caused by what the MODEL omits ⇒ fix the model.

▸ 8 · Pre-test bias: test → re-specify → test again on the same data

What it is: the diagnostic-fix loop this whole page teaches has a dark side. If you test, re-specify because of the test, and then report the final t-stats as if that spec were chosen in advance, your inference is distorted: the data picked the model AND graded it. Final "5%" tests reject true nulls far more than 5%.

How it bites: drop insignificant regressors, then declare the survivors "significant at 5%" — the survivors were selected for big t-stats, so the yardstick no longer applies. Try four functional forms, keep the one with the prettiest RESET, publish. Multiply across a profession and you get the replication crisis: factors that "worked" in-sample and died out-of-sample.

How to detect: you can't, from the final table — the damage is procedural, not visible in output. Referees ask: how many specifications were tried? Was any data held out?

Remedy: split the sample — specify on one half, test on the other; pre-register the specification when stakes are high; report the search path honestly; treat in-sample specification searches as descriptive, not confirmatory. One look = one test; ten looks ≠ ten independent tests.

▸ 9 · Heteroskedasticity as information: when the disease IS the patient

What it is: this week treats changing variance as a nuisance to be robustified away. In finance it's often the most valuable object in the data: time-varying variance IS risk. ARCH/GARCH models give ût² its own equation and forecast it.

How it bites (in reverse): an option desk, a VaR engine, or a position-sizing rule doesn't want heteroskedasticity "corrected" — it wants σt predicted. Slapping robust SEs on a returns regression and moving on throws away the strongest, most forecastable signal daily financial data contains (volatility clustering: |returns| autocorrelate out to months).

How to detect: ARCH-LM — regress ût² on its own lags; Ljung–Box on û²; or just plot ût² over time and watch it arrive in storms.

Remedy / reframe: two different diseases share a name. Variance varying with a REGRESSOR (cross-section flavour) ⇒ White/HC, this week. Variance varying over TIME in clusters ⇒ model it — GARCH, Week 7, where today's nuisance becomes the star of the show.

▸ 10 · Durbin–Watson's blind spots: the test everyone quotes and shouldn't lean on

What it is: DW only sees FIRST-order autocorrelation; and the moment a lagged dependent variable sits on the right-hand side, the statistic is biased TOWARDS 2 — i.e. towards telling you everything is fine precisely when it isn't.

How it bites: monthly data with seasonal (12th-order) error correlation sails through with d ≈ 2 — DW never looked at lag 12. A partial-adjustment model yt = a + byt−1 + cxt + ut "passes" DW by construction, the analyst relaxes, and the autocorrelation quietly biases the persistence estimate b̂ (with lagged y, correlated errors mean E[u|X] ≠ 0 — bias, not just bad SEs).

How to detect: Breusch–Godfrey with lags matched to the data frequency (12 for monthly, 4 for quarterly) — valid WITH lagged y; Ljung–Box on residuals as a quick scan; and remember d ≈ 2 only rules out lag-1 patterns.

Remedy: make BG your default and choose its lag order consciously; keep DW for what it's genuinely good at — a one-glance spurious-regression smell test (d ≪ 1 in a levels regression of trending series = Week 5's alarm bell).
