← All topics

Value-at-Risk answers one question: “How much could we lose tomorrow, on a bad day, with q% confidence?” Here you build VaR from the parametric formula, see how Student-t tails and GARCH clustering change the answer, backtest it Basel-style — then run a trading desk for ten days and find out whether the regulator smiles or frowns.

📚 Parameters & concepts

The risk manager’s vocabulary. Click a card for the formula, the numbers desks actually see, the extremes, and what goes wrong in estimation.

▾ detailscConfidence level (95% / 99%)
How deep into the tail the VaR quantile sits.
Meaning. VaR at level c is the loss exceeded only (1−c) of the time: by construction we EXPECT breaches on (1−c) of days.
Formula. P(loss > VaRc) = 1 − c.
Typical values. Basel market risk: 99% (and 97.5% for ES under FRTB); internal desk limits often 95%.
At the extremes. c → 99.9%+: the estimate hangs on a handful of observations and its variance explodes. c low (90%): plenty of breaches to count, but it says little about catastrophes.
Estimation problems. Validation starves at high c: 99% with 250 days ⇒ 2.5 expected breaches — distinguishing a good model from a bad one on 2.5 events is statistically heroic (that is why the Basel zones are so wide).
▾ detailsz, tνNormal & t quantiles
The tail multipliers: how many σ’s out the cut-off sits.
Meaning. The distributional ingredient of parametric VaR — all the disagreement between models lives here.
Formula. z0.05 = −1.645, z0.01 = −2.326; unit-variance t(ν): tν,q·√((ν−2)/ν), e.g. ν = 6 at 1%: −2.566.
Typical values. The t premium over the normal at 99% runs 10–25% for ν = 4–8 — material capital.
At the extremes. ν → ∞ recovers z. At 95% the unit-variance t quantile can sit INSIDE the normal one — fat tails only bite in the far tail.
Estimation problems. Forgetting the √((ν−2)/ν) rescaling double-counts fat tails (σ would no longer mean the standard deviation); ν̂ itself is pinned down by a few extreme days.
▾ detailsσ̂t+1Volatility forecast
Tomorrow’s conditional σ — the engine room of any VaR number.
Meaning. VaR scales one-for-one with σ, so the volatility model IS most of the VaR model.
Formula. From GARCH: σ²t+1 = ω + αr²t + βσ²t; RiskMetrics: σ²t+1 = 0.06r²t + 0.94σ²t.
Typical values. Equity index: 0.5–1.5% per day in calm markets, 4–8% in crises — an 8× swing that static models simply miss.
At the extremes. Static σ̂ (full-sample sd): breaches cluster violently in storms (Platform 2 proves it). Over-reactive σ̂: capital requirements whipsaw.
Estimation problems. All of week 7’s problems flow downstream into VaR: persistence bias from breaks, ω̂ ≈ 0, normal tails — risk numbers inherit every one.
▾ detailsVaRValue-at-Risk
“With c% confidence, we will not lose more than THIS tomorrow.”
Meaning. A quantile of the P&L distribution, translated into money. The lingua franca of market-risk limits since the 1990s.
Formula. Parametric: VaRc = −(μ + z1−cσ)·V. Historical simulation: the empirical (1−c) quantile of past returns × V.
Typical values. A diversified equity desk: 1-day 99% VaR ≈ 2–4% of book value.
At the extremes. VaR is NOT subadditive: merging two desks can RAISE measured risk — a pathological property for a risk measure (ES fixes this).
Estimation problems. It says nothing about how bad the bad day is — two desks with identical VaR can hide wildly different catastrophes (Platform 3 shows the trick).
▾ detailsESExpected Shortfall
The AVERAGE loss on the days VaR is breached — “how bad is bad?”.
Meaning. The conditional mean of the tail beyond VaR. Coherent (subadditive), tail-sensitive, and now the Basel standard.
Formula. ESc = E[loss | loss > VaRc]; normal case: ES = σ·φ(zq)/q − μ, so ES99 = 2.665σ vs VaR99 = 2.326σ.
Typical values. ES97.5 ≈ VaR99 under normality — exactly why Basel’s FRTB chose 97.5%.
At the extremes. Fat tails push ES/VaR far above its normal ratio of ≈ 1.15; for very heavy tails ES may not even exist.
Estimation problems. Needs more tail data than VaR; backtesting it is subtler (elicitability debate) — the standard answer is to backtest VaR levels alongside.
▾ detailsxBreach / exception
A day when the realised loss exceeds the VaR — the unit of backtesting.
Meaning. The hit sequence It = 1[losst > VaRt] should be iid Bernoulli(1−c): the right NUMBER of breaches, at UNPREDICTABLE times.
Formula. E[x] = n(1−c): 2.5 breaches per 250 days at 99%.
Typical values. Static-σ models in clustered markets: the right count over years, delivered in one terrible fortnight.
At the extremes. x = 0 over years is itself a failure — an over-conservative model wasting capital. x clustered: the independence condition is violated even if the count is fine.
Estimation problems. Counting breaches tests only the count; clustering needs an independence test (Christoffersen — see the clinic).
▾ detailsLRPOFKupiec POF test
Is the observed breach count consistent with the promised 1−c?
Meaning. A likelihood-ratio test of “proportion of failures”: were x breaches in n days plausible if the true breach probability were p = 1−c?
Formula. LR = −2ln[(1−p)n−xpx] + 2ln[(1−x/n)n−x(x/n)x] ~ χ²(1).
Typical values. At x exactly = np, LR = 0 (perfect calibration); LR > 3.84 rejects at 5%.
At the extremes. Two-sided: it also rejects too FEW breaches — with n = 250, c = 99%, x = 0 gives LR = 5.02, p ≈ 0.025: zero breaches is statistically suspicious, not virtuous.
Estimation problems. Low power in regulatory samples (250 days), and blind to clustering — pair it with an independence test before declaring victory.
▾ details🚦Basel traffic-light zones
Green ≤ 4, yellow 5–9, red ≥ 10 breaches per 250 days at 99%.
Meaning. The regulator’s pragmatic backtest: count last year’s exceptions and look up the zone. Yellow raises the capital multiplier from 3 towards 4; red usually kills the model.
Formula. The zones are binomial cumulative probabilities: green covers outcomes up to the 95th percentile of Binomial(250, 0.01), yellow to 99.99% — computed, not arbitrary.
Typical values. P(x ≤ 4) ≈ 89%, so even a perfect model lands yellow ~11% of the time — supervisors expect the occasional unlucky yellow.
At the extremes. A model that under-reports risk by 25% still has a good chance of staying green for a year — the test’s power is weak by design (small n).
Estimation problems. One year of data per verdict; clustering ignored; and the multiplier mechanics give desks an incentive to game the boundary (clinic, card 5).

📋 Backtest: would this VaR have survived the last 250 trading days?

We simulate 250 days of GARCH(1,1) returns (α = 0.08, β = 0.89 — realistic clustering, same unconditional σ and μ as above, Gaussian shocks) and count how often the loss exceeded your fixed VaR.

What to notice.
🧪 Try this.
  1. 99% with Student-t, ν = 3: the fattest tails the slider allows — watch the t premium over the normal VaR. This is the difference between surviving 2008 and not.
  2. Switch the same setup to 95%: the unit-variance t VaR can fall BELOW the normal one. Fat tails are a far-tail phenomenon — mid-tail they actually give some probability back.
  3. Crisis volatility: σ = 4%. VaR scales linearly in σ — whoever forecasts σ controls the VaR. The distribution choice is the garnish; the vol model is the meal.
  4. Run the 250-day backtest five times and watch WHERE the breaches land: almost always bunched in the stormy stretch. The count can pass while the timing screams model failure.

Backtest lab — four VaR models vs a 3-year GARCH-t market

We simulate 3 trading years (750 days, after a 260-day warm-up) of a market with real-world features: GARCH volatility (α = 0.09, β = 0.89, σ̄ = 1.2%/day) and Student-t(6) shocks. Four risk desks then publish daily VaR: a static normal (σ estimated once from the warm-up year), a normal-GARCH (perfect σₜ, wrong tails), a t-GARCH (the well-specified model), and historical simulation (rolling 250-day empirical quantile). Kupiec’s LR = −2ln[(1−p)ⁿ⁻ˣpˣ] + 2ln[(1−x/n)ⁿ⁻ˣ(x/n)ˣ] ~ χ²(1) judges each count; the traffic light applies the Basel zones to the most recent 250 days.

What to notice.
🧪 Try this.
  1. Chart the static normal: its VaR line is dead flat, so every volatility storm produces a burst of breaches. It often passes 95% and fails 99% — tails AND clustering both bite at the deep quantile.
  2. Chart the t-GARCH: the VaR line breathes with the market and the breaches scatter like rain — roughly the right number, at unpredictable times. That is what “well-specified” looks like.
  3. Chart historical simulation and find the step pattern: after a calm year its VaR is tiny, and the first week of a storm punches straight through it. Run several markets and watch the breach bursts at regime turns.
  4. Switch to 95%: with 37.5 expected breaches the Kupiec test suddenly has power — models that hid comfortably behind 7.5 expected breaches at 99% get exposed.

Square-root-of-time lab — when scaling 1-day VaR by √10 lies

Basel lets banks scale 1-day VaR to 10 days by √10 — exactly right for iid returns, quietly wrong under volatility clustering. We start a GARCH market (σ̄ = 1.2%/day, α = 0.05) from a calm, typical or crisis day, simulate 2,000 ten-day paths, and compare the √10-scaled VaR with the true simulated 10-day 99% VaR.

0.90
What to notice. Press ▶ to simulate.
🧪 Try this.
  1. Calm start, persistence 0.90: the true 10-day VaR comes out ~20–25% ABOVE the √10 rule — volatility mean-reverts UP towards σ̄ over the window, and the rule never sees it coming.
  2. Crisis start, persistence 0.90: now the rule OVERSTATES — vol decays towards σ̄ inside the 10 days (though vol-of-vol fat tails claw some back).
  3. Persistence 0: iid returns — the rule is almost exact (ratio ≈ 1). √time scaling is an iid theorem, not a law of nature.
  4. So is √10 scaling conservative or reckless? Answer: it depends on where you START — which is precisely why regulators increasingly demand full 10-day simulation instead.

⚠️ Problems clinic

Six classic VaR failures. Click each card; three carry live demos.

▾ openVaR says nothing beyond the quantile
Two books, identical VaR, catastrophically different tails.
What it is. VaR is one point on the loss distribution. Everything past it — the size of the disaster — is invisible by construction.
How it bites. Selling deep out-of-the-money puts books steady premium income, leaves VaR untouched, and concentrates ruin just past the quantile. Pre-2008 CDO books had exactly this shape.
How to detect. Report ES next to VaR and watch the ratio: ES/VaR drifting up means tail mass is being parked beyond the cut-off. Stress tests and max-loss scenarios catch what quantiles cannot.
Remedy. ES as the headline measure (as FRTB now requires), scenario limits, and payoff-shape review for anything optionable. Platform 3’s ⚖️ demo is this card in chart form.
▾ openBreach clustering (independence violated)
The right NUMBER of breaches — all in the same fortnight.
What it is. Correct unconditional coverage but dependent hits: P(breach today | breach yesterday) ≫ P(breach today | calm yesterday). Static-σ models in GARCH markets fail exactly this way.
How it bites. Five breaches scattered over a year are survivable; five in ten days exhaust liquidity, trigger margin calls and end desks — with the SAME Kupiec statistic.
How to detect. Christoffersen’s independence test: build the 2×2 transition matrix of the hit sequence and LR-test whether P(hit|hit) = P(hit|no hit); or simply chart the breach dates (demo).
Remedy. Conditional VaR — let σₜ come from a GARCH/EWMA filter so the VaR line breathes with the market; then the hits decluster.
▾ openHistorical-simulation window staleness
HS-VaR is a rear-view mirror with a 250-day memory.
What it is. The empirical quantile only moves when observations enter or leave the window: it reacts to a crisis AFTER the losses arrive, and forgets it abruptly 250 days later (“ghost effects”).
How it bites. On the eve of a vol regime change, HS-VaR is calibrated to the dead calm regime — the first week of the storm blows straight through it. A year later VaR halves overnight as the crisis exits the window, just as memories fade.
How to detect. A stepped, plateau-shaped VaR series; breach bursts concentrated immediately after regime turns (Platform 2, model 4).
Remedy. Filtered HS (scale past returns by σ̂ₜ/σ̂ₜ₋ⱼ), exponentially weighted quantiles, or shorter windows with a vol floor.
▾ openNormality in stressed markets
The tails fatten and the correlations go to 1 — exactly when it matters.
What it is. Gaussian VaR assumes thin tails and stable dependence. Crises deliver fat tails, skew, and diversification that evaporates as everything sells off together.
How it bites. “We were seeing 25-standard-deviation moves, several days in a row” (Goldman’s CFO, August 2007). Under normality one 8σ day occurs once per several lifetimes of the universe; the model, not the universe, was wrong.
How to detect. QQ-plots of standardised residuals; breach counts at 99% far above 1% while 95% looks fine (a tail-shape signature); kurtosis ≫ 3.
Remedy. Student-t or EVT tails, stressed calibration windows (Basel’s stressed VaR/ES), and scenario analysis that does not trust estimated correlations.
▾ openGaming the VaR
Whatever you measure, traders will optimise. VaR is very gameable.
What it is. Goodhart’s law on a trading floor: positions engineered to look small in VaR terms — risk tucked just beyond the quantile, short-vol carry trades, basis positions whose risk is in the spread the model ignores.
How it bites. The desk’s reported VaR is flat while its true exposure compounds; the blow-up, when it comes, is “outside the model”. Bonus formulas based on return-over-VaR actively reward this.
How to detect. ES/VaR ratio drifting upward; P&L distributions with steady small gains and rare huge losses (negative skew); positions concentrated in instruments the model maps crudely.
Remedy. Multiple measures (ES, stress, notional and concentration limits), model-risk review of mapping choices, and compensation that claws back tail losses.
▾ openExpect breaches! (2.5 per year is the target)
Zero breaches is not prudence — it is mispriced capital.
What it is. At 99% over 250 days the DESIGN target is 2.5 exceptions. A correctly calibrated model breaches regularly, on schedule, in small doses.
How it bites. A desk reporting zero breaches for three years is either extraordinarily lucky (P(x = 0) = 0.99²⁵⁰ ≈ 8% per year) or running VaR far above the true quantile — idle capital that costs basis points every day and strangles the business.
How to detect. Kupiec is two-sided: x = 0 with n = 250 gives LR = 5.02, p ≈ 0.025 — statistically rejectable over-conservatism. The binomial demo shows the full distribution of honest breach counts.
Remedy. Backtest for too FEW breaches as seriously as for too many; track capital efficiency next to safety; remember the traffic light’s green zone starts at zero but its sweet spot is 2–3.
