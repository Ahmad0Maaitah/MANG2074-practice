📚 Parameters & concepts

Click a card to expand. The first six numbers are the entire vocabulary of this week — a risk desk quotes them daily. The last five cards are the ideas that connect them.

μ — drift
The slow average pull on prices — mean return per day.

Meaning: the expected return per period. Over a year of daily data the price drifts by roughly 252·μ.

Formula: rt = μ + σ·zt; μ̂ = r̄ with SE(μ̂) = σ/√n.

Typical values: equities ≈ +0.02 to +0.05%/day (≈5–12%/yr); FX majors and cash ≈ 0; a daily Sharpe |μ/σ| above ~0.1 should make you suspicious of the data.

At the extremes: μ = 0 → prices are a fair game (martingale); μ < 0 → persistent bleed (leveraged-ETF decay, futures roll costs); μ large relative to σ basically never happens at daily frequency.

Estimation problems: noise dominates — with σ = 1%/day you need decades before μ̂ is statistically distinguishable from zero. Drift is the hardest number in finance to estimate.

σ — volatility
The typical size of a daily move; risk's first name.

Meaning: the standard deviation of returns — how big a "normal" day is.

Formula: σ̂² = Σ(rt − r̄)²/(n−1); annualised σann = σdaily·√252 (if returns are iid!).

Typical values: calm large-cap equities 1–2%/day, FX majors 0.5–0.8%, crypto 4–8%, index in a crisis 5%+. Annualised: equities ~15–30%, crypto ~80–150%.

At the extremes: σ → 0 — pegged FX or a bond at maturity; doubling σ quadruples variance; in real markets σ is itself time-varying (volatility clustering) — that's Week 7's GARCH.

Estimation problems: σ̂ is precise under iid, but clustering makes the usual SE wrong; a single bad tick or stock split error inflates it badly.

S — skewness
Asymmetry: are crashes bigger than rallies?

Meaning: the tilt of the distribution. Negative S = the big moves are mostly down — typical for equity indices.

Formula: S = E[(r−μ)³]/σ³; feeds Jarque–Bera through S².

Typical values: daily index returns S ≈ −0.5 to 0; single stocks near 0; option-selling strategies strongly negative ("picking up pennies in front of a steamroller").

At the extremes: S = 0 symmetric; S < −1 crash-prone; strongly positive = lottery-like payoffs (penny stocks, crypto pumps).

Estimation problems: cubed deviations mean ONE outlier can dominate the whole estimate; converges very slowly — SE(Ŝ) ≈ √(6/n) under normality.

K — excess kurtosis
Fat tails: extreme days happen far more often than the normal allows.

Meaning: tail weight beyond the Gaussian benchmark. K > 0 = "leptokurtic" — quiet most days, occasionally wild.

Formula: K = E[(r−μ)⁴]/σ⁴ − 3; feeds JB through K²/4.

Typical values: daily equity returns K ≈ 3–10; weekly smaller, monthly smaller still ("aggregational Gaussianity"); crypto and crisis periods much higher.

At the extremes: K = 0 → normal; for a Student-t, K = 6/(ν−4), which is infinite for ν ≤ 4. A 1987-style −20σ day is "impossible" under normality but merely rare with fat tails.

Estimation problems: fourth powers converge even more slowly than third — SE(K̂) ≈ √(24/n) under normality, and the sample estimate is badly biased downward in small samples.

ν — t degrees of freedom
The tail-thickness dial of the Student-t: smaller ν = fatter tails.

Meaning: the one-parameter knob controlling how heavy the tails of a t-distribution are.

Formula: Var = ν/(ν−2) (so we standardise by √((ν−2)/ν)); excess kurtosis = 6/(ν−4) for ν > 4.

Typical values: ν ≈ 3–6 fits daily equity returns remarkably well; ν ≈ 4 for crypto; ν ≥ 30 is essentially Gaussian.

At the extremes: ν ≤ 2 → variance doesn't exist; ν ≤ 4 → kurtosis infinite; ν → ∞ → exactly normal.

Estimation problems: ML estimates of ν have wide confidence intervals — identification comes only from the rare tail observations, and unmodelled GARCH masquerades as low ν.

n — sample size
How much data you have — and whose regime it came from.

Meaning: the number of return observations. Everything asymptotic (JB, CLT, SEs) leans on n.

Formula: JB = n/6·(S² + K²/4) ~ χ²(2); SEs shrink at rate 1/√n.

Typical values: 1 year daily ≈ 252; 5 years ≈ 1260; 10 years monthly = 120 (tiny!); intraday data — thousands per day.

At the extremes: small n → JB unreliable and powerless (see the testing bench); huge n → JB rejects everything, because no real series is exactly normal — statistical vs economic significance.

Estimation problems: stretching n back decades mixes regimes — 1990s data may say little about today's market structure. More data is only better if the DGP hasn't changed.

Σr — log vs simple aggregation
Log returns add over TIME; simple returns add across a PORTFOLIO.

Meaning: the two return definitions aggregate in opposite directions. A 20-day log return is the simple sum of 20 daily log returns; a portfolio's simple return is the weight-averaged simple return of its holdings. Neither works the other way round.

Formula: rlog0→T = Σt rlogt = ln(PT/P0); Rp = Σi wiRi (simple only); conversion R = er − 1.

Typical values: at daily ±1% moves, |rlog − R| < 0.005% — invisible. At a ±10% day the gap is ~0.5%; at −50% it is 19.3 percentage points (ln 0.5 = −69.3%).

At the extremes: R → −100% gives rlog → −∞ (logs "know" you can't lose more than everything); summing SIMPLE returns over time treats −50% then +50% as flat when you actually lost 25%.

Estimation problems: the classic backtest bug is compounding simple returns additively (or log returns multiplicatively). At crypto volatilities the error compounds into fantasy performance. Verify Σrlog = ln(PT/P0) holds to machine precision — Platform 6 does it live.

QQ — quantile–quantile anatomy
One picture, three diagnoses: straight = normal, S = fat tails, banana = skew.

Meaning: plot your sorted (standardised) sample against the quantiles a normal would produce for the same probability levels. Where the data have "more extreme" quantiles than the normal, the dots leave the 45° line.

Formula: point i has x = Φ−1((i − 0.5)/n) and y = z(i), the i-th order statistic of (r − r̄)/σ̂.

Typical values: daily equity/FX/crypto returns show the S-shape — left tail below the line, right tail above (both tails too heavy). A pure skew DGP bends like a banana: one tail off, the other on. Real index data: S-shape plus extra left-tail droop.

At the extremes: a single point far off the line with everything else straight = one outlier/bad print, not a fat-tailed DGP; dots that snap to horizontal steps = stale or rounded prices.

Estimation problems: the last 2–3 dots in each tail are estimated from a handful of observations — even truly normal samples wiggle there (try n = 150 in Platform 5). Read the pattern of the outer 5–10%, never individual dots.

K vs P(|r| > kσ) — kurtosis vs tails
Kurtosis is a moment; tail risk is a probability. They are cousins, not twins.

Meaning: K summarises the 4th moment — an average of z⁴ — while what a risk desk actually fears is the exceedance probability P(|r| > kσ). High K usually means heavy tails, but K can be bought by one observation or by a fat "shoulder" with modest tails.

Formula: K + 3 = E[z⁴]; normal tail P(|z| > k) = 2Φ(−k): 4.55% (k=2), 0.27% (k=3), 0.0063% (k=4), 0.000057% (k=5).

Typical values: daily index data deliver |r| > 3σ roughly 3–5× the normal rate and |r| > 5σ hundreds of times the normal rate — even when sample K is a "modest" 5.

At the extremes: for t(ν≤4) the population K is infinite, yet every sample gives a finite K̂ — the estimate never converges, it just grows with n. Conversely, normal-plus-one-outlier gives huge K̂ with genuinely thin tails.

Estimation problems: K̂ is the most fragile statistic in this course: dominated by the few largest |r|, biased down in small samples, SE ≈ √(24/n) only under normality (useless exactly when you need it). Count exceedances directly (Platform 7) instead of trusting one number.

CLT — aggregational Gaussianity
Fat-tailed daily returns become almost normal monthly — the CLT grinding slowly.

Meaning: a monthly return is the sum of ~21 daily log returns. Sums of (weakly dependent) draws drift towards the normal, so the longer the horizon, the thinner the standardised tails — a stylised fact of essentially every asset class.

Formula: for iid sums of m periods, Km = K1/m and Sm = S1/√m; Var scales linearly (σm = σ1√m), which is where ×√252 comes from.

Typical values: equity index excess kurtosis ≈ 5–10 daily, ≈ 1–3 weekly, ≈ 0.5–1.5 monthly, near 0 quarterly. JB rejects daily data with astronomical statistics, often cannot reject quarterly.

At the extremes: convergence is SLOW because returns aren't iid — volatility clustering makes effective m much smaller than 21; for ν ≤ 4 tails the textbook K1/m rule doesn't even apply (K1 = ∞). Intraday data are wildly non-normal; annual data look almost Gaussian.

Estimation problems: the kindness is paid for in sample size — 10 years is 2520 daily but only 40 quarterly observations, so monthly/quarterly moments are noisy. Monthly econometrics is "kinder" distributionally but poorer statistically. See Platform 6.

JB — Jarque–Bera mechanics
JB = n/6·(S² + K²/4) ~ χ²(2): a moment-matching tribunal with a sample-size problem.

Meaning: under H₀ (normality), Ŝ ≈ N(0, 6/n) and K̂ ≈ N(0, 24/n), so JB is just the sum of two squared z-scores — hence χ² with 2 degrees of freedom. It asks only "are the 3rd and 4th moments Gaussian?", nothing about the tails directly.

Formula: JB = n/6·(S² + (Kraw − 3)²/4); reject at 5% if JB > 5.99 (χ²₂ critical value); p = e−JB/2.

Typical values: 10 years of daily index returns: JB in the hundreds or thousands (p ≈ 0). Quarterly returns: often single digits. Truly normal data: JB ≈ 2 on average (the mean of a χ²₂).

At the extremes: power grows linearly in n — at n = 50,000 (20 years of minutes) JB rejects for S = 0.03, a deviation of zero economic consequence. Huge n turns JB into a machine that rejects everything; tiny n turns it into one that rejects nothing.

Estimation problems: the χ²(2) law is asymptotic — actual size drifts from 5% below n ≈ 100 (clinic card 1); one outlier inflates both S² and K² and convicts an innocent normal; volatility clustering violates the iid assumption behind the 6/n and 24/n variances, so real-data JB overstates the evidence.

⚠️ Problems clinic

Nine classic ways this week's tools go wrong in practice. Click to open each case.

▸ 1 · Small-sample JB: an unreliable witness

What it is: JB's χ²(2) distribution is only an asymptotic approximation. In small samples the true distribution of JB is far from χ²(2): the actual rejection rate at the "5%" critical value drifts away from 5%, and power against real alternatives is weak.

How it bites: a student tests one year of monthly fund returns (n = 12!) and reports "JB does not reject — returns are normal, so Gaussian VaR is fine". The test never had a chance: SE(Ŝ) ≈ √(6/12) ≈ 0.71 and SE(K̂) ≈ √(24/12) ≈ 1.41 — enormous.

How to detect: simple — look at n. Below n ≈ 100, treat JB p-values as decoration. Run the demo below to see the actual size at n = 30.

Remedy: get more data (use daily, not monthly), use Monte-Carlo/bootstrap critical values, and never base a distributional claim on a single test in a small sample.

▸ 2 · Simple vs log returns: fine until the market isn't

What it is: rlog = ln(1+R) ≈ R only for small moves. Log returns add over time; simple returns add over a portfolio. Mixing them up is harmless at ±1% and catastrophic at ±50%.

How it bites: a coin drops 50% then gains 50%: sum of simple returns = 0%, but you've lost 25%. In logs: −69.3% + 40.5% = −28.8% = ln(0.75). A backtest that compounds simple returns as if they were logs overstates crypto performance massively.

How to detect: any |return| above ~15–20% in the sample means the gap is material. Check: does Σrlog equal ln(PT/P0)? It must, exactly.

Remedy: use log returns for time-series modelling and aggregation over time; convert to simple (R = er−1) for portfolio weights and P&L statements.

▸ 3 · Volatility clustering: returns are not iid

What it is: real returns are (nearly) serially uncorrelated, but their squares are strongly autocorrelated — quiet weeks cluster, turbulent weeks cluster. The iid assumption behind √n standard errors, √252 scaling and the JB null is false.

How it bites: a VaR model calibrated in a calm spell understates risk just before the storm; "20-day vol" jumps 4× in a crisis. The marginal distribution looks fat-tailed even when each day's shock is conditionally normal.

How to detect: plot the ACF of squared (or absolute) returns — significant spikes for many lags; Ljung–Box test on r²; Engle's ARCH-LM test (Week 7).

Remedy: model the variance: ARCH/GARCH (Week 7), or at minimum use heteroscedasticity-robust inference and rolling-window volatility.

▸ 4 · Survivorship bias & outliers: poisoned moments

What it is: datasets that quietly drop dead companies (or one fat-fingered data error) distort every moment you compute. μ̂ inflates (only winners survive), while S and K — built from cubed and fourth-powered deviations — can be dominated by a single observation.

How it bites: backtesting on today's S&P 500 constituents overstates historical returns by 1–4%/yr; one bad tick of −20% turns a tame series into a "fat-tailed" one and flips the JB verdict.

How to detect: rank |returns| and inspect the top few by hand (real event or data error?); recompute S, K with the single largest observation removed — if the answer changes materially, you have an outlier problem. For survivorship: compare against a point-in-time constituent list.

Remedy: vet extreme observations against news; use point-in-time databases (CRSP with delistings); winsorise only with disclosure — an honest fat tail is information, not noise.

▸ 5 · Annualising with ×√252: terms and conditions apply

What it is: σann = σdaily·√252 assumes returns are iid. The rule is frequency-specific (√12 for monthly, √52 for weekly, √365 for crypto) and breaks when returns are autocorrelated or volatility clusters.

How it bites: positively autocorrelated returns (illiquid assets, smoothed fund NAVs) make √252 understate true annual risk — hedge funds love this. And scaling a fat-tailed daily 1% VaR by √h is plain wrong: tail quantiles don't scale like standard deviations.

How to detect: variance-ratio test — compare Var(20-day return) with 20×Var(daily return); equality holds only under iid. Check the return ACF at lag 1.

Remedy: compute risk at the horizon you care about directly from data at that horizon, or model the dynamics (GARCH) and aggregate properly.

▸ 6 · One bad print or a genuinely fat tail? The winsorising debate

What it is: a −15% observation can be (a) a data error — fat finger, missed stock split, bad close — or (b) the heavy-tailed DGP doing exactly what it does. Card 4 showed that one point owns S and K; this card is about deciding which, because the remedies are opposite: errors must be removed, real tail events must be KEPT.

How it bites: a risk team "cleans" its sample by winsorising at ±3σ — and its VaR model then promises that 5σ days don't exist, right up until one arrives. The opposite error: leaving a stock-split artefact in (a fake −50% "return") makes every moment, beta and VaR garbage. Both mistakes look like diligence in a methods section.

How to detect: investigate each extreme by hand: (1) does the date match a real event (earnings, 2008, COVID, a flash crash)? (2) does the same move appear in another data vendor / in volume data? (3) does the price reverse 100% the next day (classic bad print) or behave like news? A QQ plot helps: a heavy-tailed DGP bends the whole tail smoothly; a single error is one isolated dot off an otherwise straight line.

Remedy: correct verified errors at the source; never winsorise silently. If you must trim/winsorise for a moment-sensitive estimator, report results both ways. In finance the tail IS the phenomenon — deleting it is deleting the risk you are paid to measure. (Cross-check: clinic card 4's demo, and Platform 5's outlier shape.)

▸ 7 · Survivorship bias: the dead don't report returns

What it is: databases and benchmarks quietly drop funds that closed and stocks that delisted. The sample you download is conditioned on survival — its mean is biased UP and its left tail is artificially thin, because the worst trajectories (the ones that died) are exactly the ones missing.

How it bites: hedge-fund index returns are overstated by roughly 1–4%/yr in academic estimates once dead funds are restored; a strategy backtested on today's S&P 500 members "discovers" that buying dips always worked — for the firms that survived to be in today's index. Distribution work suffers too: skewness looks less negative and crash frequencies look lower than the investable reality.

How to detect: ask the database: are dead/delisted entities included (CRSP delisting returns, "graveyard" files in TASS/Morningstar)? Red flags: no fund in the sample ever reports a terminal −100%-ish episode; the constituent list is dated TODAY but the returns start in 1995.

Remedy: use point-in-time constituents and survivorship-bias-free files (include delisting returns); for fund data, merge live + graveyard databases; at minimum, state the bias direction — your mean is an upper bound, your left-tail risk a lower bound.

▸ 8 · Stale prices & zero returns: the illiquidity illusion

What it is: illiquid assets (small caps, corporate bonds, real estate, some fund NAVs) don't trade every day. The recorded price is then yesterday's — the return series fills with zeros, and true moves get smeared across several observed days. Measured volatility is too LOW and the series shows positive autocorrelation that the underlying asset doesn't have.

How it bites: a private-asset or small-cap portfolio looks beautifully low-risk and high-Sharpe next to listed equities — until you need to sell. Smoothed NAVs understate σ by 30–50% in some studies; the same staleness creates spurious lag-1 autocorrelation that a naive student reads as "predictability" (and a Week 2 regression happily t-tests it). The √252 rule then under-annualises risk on top (card 5).

How to detect: count zero returns — more than a few % of exact zeros in daily data is a tell; check the lag-1 ACF of returns (illiquid series: +0.1 to +0.4); compare vol measured daily vs from non-overlapping monthly returns — staleness makes the daily number much smaller.

Remedy: sample at a frequency at which the asset actually trades; unsmooth NAV series (e.g. Geltner/Getmansky-type corrections); for risk, use the variance of longer-horizon returns rather than scaled daily σ̂.

▸ 9 · Annualising the MEAN: ×252 traps

What it is: card 5 covered ×√252 for volatility; this is its sibling for the mean. Multiplying the average daily LOG return by 252 is legitimate (logs add over time, so the expected annual log return is exactly 252·μlog). Multiplying the average daily SIMPLE return by 252 is not a sum of anything — simple returns compound, they don't add.

How it bites: a fund with mean daily simple return 0.04% and σ = 2% advertises "0.04% × 252 ≈ 10% p.a.". The compounded (geometric) reality is ≈ (252·(R̄ − σ²/2)) in log terms ≈ 252·(0.0004 − 0.0002) ≈ 5% — half the headline. The gap, ≈ σ²/2 per period ("volatility drag"), is why leveraged ETFs bleed and why arithmetic backtest means flatter every volatile strategy.

How to detect: recompute the annual figure from first principles: does (PT/P0)252/n − 1 match the quoted annual return? If someone quotes 252·R̄ for a volatile asset, it won't. Check whether the paper says which return definition it annualised — many don't.

Remedy: annualise the mean of LOG returns by ×252, then convert via er − 1 if you need a simple figure; or report geometric mean returns directly. Never ×252 a mean simple return except as an explicit arithmetic (non-compounding) convention — and say so.
